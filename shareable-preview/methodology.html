Project Method

Methodology

The project evaluates prophecy claims with a consistent set of criteria rather than relying on isolated proof texts. The aim is to ask how strong a claim is under scrutiny, not simply whether it has ever been made.

Six evaluation criteria

  1. Prior

    The text must clearly predate the event being cited as its fulfillment. If a passage is later than the event, or if its dating is too uncertain, the prophetic force of the claim weakens immediately.

  2. Predictive Intent

    The passage should function as a forward-looking statement rather than only as poetry, lament, narrative, or symbolic action later reused by interpreters. A text can be meaningful without being predictive in the strict sense.

  3. Non-Mundane

    The claim should describe something more than a broad or easily repeated pattern. The more ordinary or general the wording is, the easier it becomes to fit many unrelated events to it.

  4. Specific and Verifiable

    The passage should point to a reasonably identifiable event, figure, or circumstance. If multiple timelines, referents, or fulfillment candidates work equally well, the evidential value drops.

  5. Clarity

    The wording should be clear enough that the alleged meaning can be recognized without importing too much later theology. Heavy ambiguity, symbolism, or multiple viable readings make the claim harder to score strongly.

  6. Independence

    The fulfillment should not depend mainly on deliberate action, narrative shaping, or retrospective scriptural framing by those making the claim. The more the later account appears to be built around the earlier text, the more caution is needed.

Page status and confidence

Flagship Analysis marks the most developed passage pages. Reviewed Analysis marks solid but not fully developed work. Draft Analysis marks early-stage pages that may still be uneven or incomplete.

High confidence indicates strong textual grounding and balance. Moderate confidence indicates a contested or partly developed page. Low confidence indicates exploratory work that should be read provisionally.

What the project is doing

This project is evaluating the strength of prophecy claims as claims. It compares traditional Christian readings, skeptical objections, and the literary and historical context of each passage.

What the project is not doing

This is not an attempt to argue for or against Christianity as a whole. It is a narrower inquiry into how persuasive specific prophecy arguments are once the relevant context and criteria are brought into view.
