351 Prophecies Project

This project is a structured examination of the claim that Jesus fulfills Old Testament prophecy. Rather than treating that claim as obvious or impossible in advance, it asks how strong particular passages look when they are read closely in context.

The central question is not simply whether a verse can be connected to Jesus, but what kind of connection is being claimed: direct prediction, typology, canonical fulfillment, or retrospective interpretation. The goal is to evaluate those claims passage by passage with consistent criteria and a balanced use of scholarship.

Why this project exists: discussions about biblical prophecy often jump straight to conclusion-level arguments. This preview is meant to slow that down and make the reasoning visible.

How to Use This Preview

A compact introduction to the larger project

In the full project, overview pages introduce an entire passage, explain why it matters, and summarize the main lines of debate. Claim pages then zoom in on narrower subclaims, such as a single verse, phrase, or alleged fulfillment detail.

This preview includes only the overview layer. It is designed for someone who wants the argument map first: what the passage says, how the New Testament uses it, where skeptics push back, and why the passage remains important anyway.

Method at a Glance

Six criteria guide the evaluation

  • Prior: does the text clearly predate the event?
  • Predictive intent: is it actually forward-looking?
  • Non-mundane: is the claim more than a generic pattern?
  • Specificity and clarity: can the alleged fulfillment be identified without forcing it?
  • Independence: does the fulfillment stand apart from deliberate scriptural shaping?

Flagship Passages

Seven passages that frame the debate

Genesis 3:15

The earliest and most programmatic Christian reading of messianic conflict, often treated as a first hint of the defeat of evil. It matters less as a narrow prediction than as a test case for typological and canonical readings.

Isaiah 7:14

A central birth-prophecy text in Christian interpretation, but one rooted in the immediate political crisis of Ahaz. The debate turns on context, the meaning of almah, and Matthew’s explicit reuse of the verse.

Isaiah 53

The most discussed servant passage in the project, read by Christians as a portrait of suffering, death, and vindication. Its force is matched by major disagreement over the identity of the servant and the kind of fulfillment involved.

Psalm 22

A lament psalm whose language echoes through the Gospel passion narratives. It is powerful in cumulative effect, yet debated because its original form is poetic prayer rather than explicit prophecy.

Micah 5:2

One of the strongest-looking examples of apparent specificity because it names Bethlehem. The central question is whether that reference predicts a literal birthplace or evokes Davidic identity and royal restoration more broadly.

Daniel 9

The project’s major chronological test case, famous for the seventy weeks and the “anointed one” who is cut off. Its strength and weakness both come from the same source: a highly interpretable apocalyptic timeline.

Zechariah 11

A vivid passage about thirty pieces of silver, the temple, and the potter motif. It stands near the center of debates about whether fulfillment is predicted in advance or narratively framed through deliberate scriptural echo.

Methodology

How the project evaluates prophecy claims

The project does not assume that every traditional messianic proof text is equally strong. It asks how each claim fares when historical setting, literary genre, textual issues, New Testament usage, and skeptical objections are all kept in view at the same time.
