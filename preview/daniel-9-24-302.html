Daniel 9:24b — He would be holy

Claim

Christians sometimes argue that Daniel 9:24b points to the holiness or consecrated identity of Jesus within the seventy-weeks prophecy. In that reading, the line is folded into the larger claim that Daniel 9 culminates in the Messiah.

Skeptic Criteria Evaluation

  • Claimed failed criteria: 2, 3, 4, 5, 6
  • Brief skeptic summary:

Skeptics often regard this as one of the weaker Daniel 9 claims because the phrase is frequently understood to refer to "the most holy" in a cultic or sanctuary sense rather than to a person. On that reading, the move from Daniel 9:24 to Jesus' holiness in Luke 1:35 is highly theological and not an obvious direct prediction.

Old Testament Passage

Daniel 9:24 lists six outcomes to be accomplished within the seventy weeks. One of them is commonly translated along the lines of anointing or consecrating "the most holy," which places the phrase inside a larger restoration program involving sin, righteousness, Jerusalem, and sacred order.

Old Testament Context

The context is Daniel's prayer over exile, sin, and the fate of the holy city. The answer concerns the future of the people, the city, and what is holy within Israel's restored life. That setting gives good reason to hear the phrase cultically or temple-related before turning it into a direct statement about a messianic person.

Claimed New Testament Fulfillment

The dataset links this claim to Luke 1:35, where Jesus is identified as holy from the beginning of his life. The connection is thematic rather than explicit: Daniel 9 is not quoted in Luke, and the fulfillment claim depends on reading the climax of holiness in the prophecy as centered in Jesus.

Why a Skeptic Says It Fails

Skeptics usually object on criteria 2, 4, 5, and 6. Criterion 5 is especially important because the phrase may refer to a holy place or sacred thing rather than to a person at all. That weakens criterion 4 as well, since the proposition "he would be holy" may not be what Daniel 9 is saying in the first place. Criteria 2 and 6 follow because the Christian linkage depends on a later christological reading rather than on an independently clear prediction.

Original Language Notes

The crucial issue is the expression often rendered "most holy." In many biblical contexts, superlative holiness language refers to sanctuary space, sacred objects, or cultic status rather than to an individual. That lexical point is one reason many interpreters consider this claim more ambiguous than traditional prophecy lists suggest.

Historical and Cultural Context

In biblical and Second Temple usage, holiness language commonly attaches to temple furnishings, sacred precincts, priestly activity, and consecrated space. Since Daniel 9 is concerned with Jerusalem and the holy city, a cultic reading fits the historical setting naturally.

Jewish Interpretation

Jewish interpretation has generally not treated this line as a prediction of Jesus' holiness. It is more often read within the prophecy's concern for restoration, sanctity, and the future ordering of the holy people and city.

Christian Interpretation

Some Christian readers take the phrase personally and see it fulfilled in Jesus as the Holy One. Others acknowledge that a cultic reading is possible but still argue that the prophecy reaches its fullest meaning in Christ, who embodies and surpasses temple holiness.

Textual Issues

The main dispute is interpretive rather than text-critical. The question is not whether the phrase is present in Daniel 9, but whether it should be taken personally or cultically.

My Conclusion

This is one of the less persuasive individual Daniel 9 claims if judged by strict predictive standards. It works better as a theological extension within a Christian canonical reading than as a strong stand-alone prophecy about Jesus.
