Claim

Christians often read Psalm 22:15 as anticipating Jesus' thirst on the cross. This is one of the more focused sub-claims because the imagery of dryness and the tongue plays naturally into the passion narrative.

Scripture Text

Psalm 22:15

Skeptic Criteria Evaluation

Criteria 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 are still relevant, but the correspondence here is tighter than in several nearby claims. Skeptics nevertheless argue that thirst is a common element of suffering and that the Gospel detail may have been narrated with the psalm in view.

Old Testament Passage

Psalm 22:15 speaks of dried strength, a tongue sticking to the jaws, and being laid in the dust of death. In the psalm itself, the line combines thirst, weakness, and nearness to death.

Old Testament Context

This verse is part of the bodily-collapse sequence in Psalm 22:14-15. Its emphasis is not merely on thirst as an isolated symptom, but on total depletion.

Claimed New Testament Fulfillment

Christian readers commonly connect the line with John 19:28, where Jesus says, "I thirst." The fit is not an explicit quotation, but it is one of the more natural thematic links between Psalm 22 and the passion.

Why a Skeptic Says It Fails

A skeptic will say the verse does not predict a unique event so much as an ordinary feature of severe suffering. The Christian use is understandable, but it becomes strongest only when read as part of the whole psalmic pattern.

Original Language Notes

The dried-tongue imagery is vivid and not especially obscure. Its force is physical and poetic rather than textually uncertain.

Historical and Cultural Context

Expressions of thirst and dryness are common in lament and distress language. That conventional background explains both why the verse resonates and why it is not uniquely specific.

Jewish Interpretation

Jewish interpretation generally takes the verse as vivid suffering language within the lament. It is not commonly singled out as a messianic prediction.

Christian Interpretation

Christians often treat this as one of the more persuasive minor correspondences between Psalm 22 and the passion. It is usually read as a cumulative but meaningful detail in the crucifixion narrative.

Textual Issues

The text itself is fairly stable. The main issue is the ordinary-yet-evocative nature of the image.

My Conclusion

This is a moderately strong sub-claim because the thematic connection to Jesus' thirst is direct and memorable. Even so, it works best as one detail within the larger passion pattern rather than as an independent proof.
