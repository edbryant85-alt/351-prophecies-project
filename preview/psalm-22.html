Claim

Christians often argue that Psalm 22 predicts important aspects of Jesus’ suffering, rejection, and death. In Christian interpretation, it has become one of the central Old Testament texts for reading the crucifixion because several lines appear to echo scenes in the Gospel passion narratives. The passage remains heavily disputed because Psalm 22 is first and foremost a lament psalm, not an explicit prophetic oracle about a distant future messiah.

Skeptic Criteria Evaluation

Skeptics commonly argue that Psalm 22 fails criteria 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6. The strongest Christian case is that the Gospels resonate with the psalm so repeatedly that it becomes one of the central scriptural lenses for the crucifixion. The strongest skeptical case is that, from a historical-critical perspective, Psalm 22 remains a lament, not an explicit future prediction; much of its language is poetic and metaphorical; some details, especially verse 16, are textually disputed; and the New Testament may be framing Jesus’ suffering in light of the psalm. On that reading, Psalm 22 works less like a single precise prediction and more like a cluster of motifs later gathered into Christian passion theology. ## Old Testament Passage Psalm 22 begins with a cry of abandonment, moves through mockery, bodily distress, hostile enemies, and public humiliation, and later turns toward rescue, praise, and worldwide acknowledgment of God. The speaker describes himself as surrounded, weakened, and shamed, while enemies jeer and divide his garments. Taken as a whole, the psalm is not simply a scene of suffering but a movement from desperation to vindication and worship. ## Old Testament Context Psalm 22 belongs to the genre of lament psalm, where the speaker cries out in anguish, protests apparent abandonment, and appeals to God for deliverance. It is traditionally associated with David, though the literary function of the superscription and the historical setting of the psalm are debated. Original context matters because lament poetry is designed to voice suffering in worship, and the righteous sufferer motif is already deeply at home in Israel’s Psalter before any specifically Christian application is considered. ## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment The New Testament uses Psalm 22 in several ways. Most famously, Jesus quotes the opening line from the cross in the Synoptic passion narratives, and the Gospels also echo the psalm’s mockery scenes, bodily distress, and the division of garments. For Christian interpretation, this means Psalm 22 is not merely a background text but one of the key scriptural lenses through which the passion is narrated and understood. From a critical standpoint, that also raises the question of whether the passion is being independently reported, scripturally narrated, or both. ## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails ### C2 Predictive Intent Skeptics argue that Psalm 22 does not function in context as a forecast of a future messiah. The psalm speaks in the voice of an afflicted worshiper crying to God, and its original literary function is lament rather than prediction.

C3 Non-Mundane

Some lines are vivid, but skeptics note that mockery, bodily agony, abandonment, and enemy threats are common features of lament literature. Even where the psalm feels unusually intense, the experiences it describes are not obviously so specific or improbable that they function as unmistakable prophecy.

C4 Specific and Verifiable

Christians often point to details such as divided garments and hostile taunts, but skeptics reply that these details appear within poetic imagery rather than a straightforward future scenario. The connection to Jesus may be impressive cumulatively, yet many of the strongest fits depend on isolating a few lines while leaving the broader lament-and-vindication structure in the background.

C5 Clarity

This is one of the central objections. The psalm’s imagery is metaphorical, compressed, and shaped by Hebrew poetic conventions, which means readers can disagree over which elements are literal, which are hyperbolic, and which are symbolic.

C6 Independence

Skeptics also argue that the Gospel writers knew Psalm 22 well and may have narrated aspects of Jesus’ suffering with the psalm consciously in view. On that account, the issue is not simply whether Jesus’ death fits Psalm 22, but whether the Christian presentation of the crucifixion has already been scripturally shaped by it through deliberate literary echoing and thematic alignment. ## Original Language Notes The psalm opens with the well-known Hebrew cry “My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?”, a line whose emotional force is clear even if its theological implications are debated. Much of the psalm’s language is highly figurative, especially the animal imagery and bodily descriptions, so interpreters must decide when the speaker is using metaphor, when he is describing social humiliation, and when he is describing physical danger. The most famous textual issue appears in verse 16, where the wording behind translations such as “like a lion” and “they pierced” has major consequences for Christian apologetic use of the psalm. ## Historical and Cultural Context Psalm 22 belongs to the lament traditions of ancient Israel, where the suffering righteous person cries publicly to God and expects eventual vindication. Public shame, enemy mockery, and bodily collapse are all familiar elements in this kind of poetry, and they often function through vivid metaphor rather than through report-like narration. This is one reason crucifixion parallels are debated: the psalm can sound remarkably close to the passion story, but it does so through poetic language that was already available within Israel’s worship and literary imagination. ## Jewish Interpretation Major Jewish readings generally take Psalm 22 as a Davidic or David-like lament rather than as a direct prophecy of Jesus. In peshat terms, the psalm voices the suffering of a righteous worshiper who cries out, is mocked, and is eventually vindicated. Rabbinic and later Jewish tradition has also reused the psalm more broadly for communal suffering and exemplary righteous figures, which shows that the text was already portable and theologically generative within Judaism itself.

That means Jewish interpretation overlaps in part with skeptical historical criticism, since both stress lament genre and original literary function. But Jewish reading is not simply reductionist. It often preserves the psalm’s liturgical and theological depth, allowing it to speak to David, to Israel, and to later suffering communities without turning it into a direct prophecy of Jesus. ## Christian Interpretation Christian interpretation has long treated Psalm 22 as one of the strongest passion texts in the Old Testament. Some readers argue for direct predictive prophecy, especially where the Gospel narratives seem to align with specific details in the psalm. Others understand it more typologically or canonically, arguing that Jesus, as the righteous sufferer, fills out the pattern already present in Davidic lament and that the Gospels deliberately frame the crucifixion within that scriptural world.

Informed Christian readers are often persuaded less by any single line than by the cumulative shape of the psalm: abandonment, mockery, bodily exposure, rescue, and eventual public vindication. The strongest Christian case is therefore often cumulative and canonical, even when some Christians still speak in direct-prediction terms. ## Textual Issues The textual discussion around Psalm 22 often centers on the relationship between the Masoretic Text and the Septuagint, especially in verse 16 and the question of whether the line should be read in a way closer to “like a lion” or to “they pierced.” That difference matters because apologetic claims are often strongest when the psalm is translated in a way that appears to align closely with crucifixion imagery. More broadly, the textual tradition does not erase the psalm’s pre-Christian origin, but it does complicate claims that every key line is textually simple and prophetically decisive. ## My Conclusion Psalm 22 is one of the strongest Christian passion texts in the Old Testament because the Gospels repeatedly resonate with its language and imagery. Its main limitation is that it remains a lament psalm whose imagery is poetic, textually debated in places, and not framed as a stand-alone future oracle. The reading closest to the original textual horizon is the Jewish/contextual and historical-critical one, while the Christian reading is strongest as a cumulative, typological, and canonical interpretation. ## Sources - John Goldingay - Peter Craigie - Hans-Joachim Kraus - Brevard Childs - R.
