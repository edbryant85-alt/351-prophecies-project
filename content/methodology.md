# Methodology

This project evaluates the claim that Jesus fulfilled 351 Old Testament prophecies. Its purpose is not to flatten every passage into the same kind of evidence, nor to treat every traditional claim as equally strong. The aim is narrower and more transparent: to examine each claim in its literary context, describe how the New Testament and later Christian interpretation use it, and ask how well the claim holds under consistent standards.

## Project Purpose

The project is designed as a research framework rather than as a devotional guide or a debate brief. Many prophecy claims are traditional, cumulative, or theologically rich without functioning as strict stand-alone predictions. Others are more directly predictive but remain contested because of context, genre, or wording. A useful evaluation method therefore has to do more than ask whether Christians have connected a verse to Jesus. It must also ask what kind of connection is being claimed and how much weight that connection can reasonably bear.

That is why the project gives special attention to original context, interpretive category, and the difference between individual and cumulative force. Some pages will conclude that a claim is relatively strong. Others will conclude that it works mainly within a canonical or theological framework. Still others will conclude that the connection is weak under strict predictive standards even if it remains important in Christian tradition.

## What Counts as Prophecy

The word "prophecy" is used in more than one way in Jewish and Christian interpretation. This project therefore distinguishes three broad categories rather than assuming that every fulfillment claim is trying to do the same thing.

### Direct Prediction

This is the strongest and narrowest category. A direct prediction is a passage that appears to speak forward to a future person, event, or development in a way that is more than general theological pattern. The closer a text comes to naming a figure, defining the event, and making the future orientation clear, the stronger it is in this category.

Examples often discussed in this mode include royal or messianic texts such as Micah 5:2 or parts of Daniel 9, where the debate centers on whether the wording is sufficiently future-oriented and specific to support a predictive reading.

### Typological / Pattern Fulfillment

Some passages are not best read as single forecast statements but as recurring patterns that later readers believe reach a climactic expression in Jesus. In this category, the Old Testament text may originally concern David, Israel, the righteous sufferer, the servant, or another earlier figure, yet Christians argue that the pattern is intensified or completed in Christ.

Psalm 22 and parts of Isaiah 53 are often discussed this way. The argument is not always that the passage names Jesus in advance, but that the earlier text establishes a pattern of suffering, rejection, vindication, kingship, or deliverance that Christians see fulfilled more fully in him.

### Canonical / Theological Fulfillment

Some claims depend even more explicitly on the way Scripture is read as a whole. In these cases, a passage may acquire messianic force less through direct prediction than through later biblical development, theological synthesis, or reception within the canon. Genesis 3:15 is a useful example. It is often central in Christian theology, yet much of its force comes from later identification of the serpent with Satan and the offspring with Christ.

This category is important because many traditional fulfillment claims are real claims, but they are not all claims of the same kind. A passage may be theologically important and canonically fruitful without being a strong stand-alone prediction.

## The Six Evaluation Criteria

The project uses six recurring criteria. They are not a mechanical scorecard. They are a structured way of asking why a claim feels stronger in some cases and weaker in others.

### C1 Prior

This asks whether the text clearly predates the alleged fulfillment.

Why it matters: a fulfillment claim loses force if the wording cannot be shown to exist before the event it is supposed to predict. For most major Old Testament passages, this criterion is not the main problem, but it still matters when textual witnesses, dating, or later redaction become part of the discussion.

### C2 Predictive Intent

This asks whether the passage is functioning as a forward-looking prediction rather than as lament, narrative explanation, royal ideology, wisdom reflection, or retrospective theology.

Why it matters: a text may later be used christologically without originally being framed as a prediction. This is one of the project's central distinctions. The weaker the predictive intent, the more likely the claim belongs in typological or canonical categories rather than in strict forecast language.

### C3 Non-Mundane

This asks whether the claim concerns something unusual enough to carry evidential weight rather than something broad, common, or easily generalizable.

Why it matters: many passages describe suffering, rejection, opposition, kingship, or deliverance in ways that are meaningful but not uniquely surprising. A claim becomes stronger when it involves a feature that is less likely to fit many ordinary situations.

### C4 Specific and Verifiable

This asks whether the text points to a reasonably identifiable event, figure, act, or circumstance that can be assessed in history.

Why it matters: the more open-ended a passage is, the easier it is to match retrospectively. Specificity does not require modern precision, but a strong claim should give the reader something more than a vague thematic resemblance.

### C5 Clarity

This asks how stable the passage's meaning is. Are the wording, imagery, and referent relatively clear, or are they heavily poetic, symbolic, or disputed?

Why it matters: a claim may be ancient and important yet still too ambiguous to function as a clear prediction. This criterion is especially important in poetry, apocalyptic material, and passages with multiple plausible referents.

### C6 Independence

This asks whether the alleged fulfillment stands apart from the interpretive tradition that connects it to the earlier text, or whether the later account may itself be shaped by that text.

Why it matters: if New Testament authors knew a passage well and narrated Jesus in conscious relation to it, the issue is not simply "Did this happen?" but also "How much is the reported fulfillment already scripturally framed?" This criterion is often decisive in Gospel passion material and other highly literate reuse of Scripture.

## How Criteria Are Applied

The criteria are applied selectively rather than mechanically. Not every page needs equal discussion of all six. Some passages are clearly prior to Christianity, so criterion 1 matters little. Others stand or fall mainly on clarity, predictive intent, or independence. A good evaluation therefore identifies the live issues instead of pretending that every text faces the same test.

This also means the project does not produce a single numeric score for each page. That would suggest more precision than the material can bear. Instead, pages identify the most relevant objections, explain why those objections matter, and then describe the strongest interpretive category for the claim. In practice, that usually means asking questions such as these:

- Is the main problem context?
- Is the main problem poetic ambiguity?
- Is the main problem weak specificity?
- Is the main problem New Testament narrative shaping?

The answer will differ from passage to passage. Isaiah 53, Psalm 22, Micah 5:2, and Genesis 3:15 do not present the same type of case, so they should not be judged as though they were interchangeable.

## Page Status and Confidence

The project now uses a page status system so readers can tell at a glance which pages represent the strongest current work and which remain provisional.

### Page Status Levels

#### Flagship Analysis

This label is reserved for a small set of fully developed passage pages that represent the project's strongest current work. A flagship page is expected to be comparatively mature, balanced across major interpretive perspectives, and substantial enough to function as a model for the rest of the site. Where relevant, these pages also include broader full-passage scope rather than only a narrow subclaim.

#### Reviewed Analysis

This label marks pages that are solid and usable, but not yet as developed as the flagship set. A reviewed page usually has a coherent argument and reasonable balance, but it may still lack fuller sourcing depth, comparative polish, or broader contextual development.

#### Draft Analysis

This label marks early-stage pages. A draft page may still be informative, but it should be treated as provisional. These entries may remain uneven in sourcing, brevity, or interpretive development while the broader project is still being built out.

### Confidence Levels

#### High

High confidence indicates that a page is textually grounded, comparatively well balanced, and clear about the strengths and weaknesses of the claim being discussed. This does not mean the passage is undisputed; it means the page itself is mature and responsibly framed.

#### Moderate

Moderate confidence indicates that the page is reasonable and useful, but still somewhat contested, incomplete, or dependent on interpretive judgments that could be developed further. Many supporting claim pages fall into this category.

#### Low

Low confidence indicates that the page is exploratory, underdeveloped, or attached to a claim whose wording or evidence remains especially uncertain. A low-confidence page should be read as part of the project's working draft layer rather than as settled analysis.

### Last Updated and Scope

Each prophecy page now includes a `Last Updated` field so readers can see when the page was most recently revised. Flagship overview pages may also include `Scope: Full Passage Analysis` to signal that they are intended to handle the passage at the overview level rather than only as a narrow claim fragment.

## Individual vs Cumulative Claims

Some prophecy claims are relatively weak on their own but stronger when read alongside related texts. Others genuinely depend on cumulative logic and should say so openly. This project therefore distinguishes between individual claim strength and cumulative theological force.

An individual claim asks whether one passage, read on its own terms, provides a strong case. A cumulative claim asks whether several passages together create a larger pattern that Christians reasonably see fulfilled in Jesus. Both kinds of arguments exist in the tradition, but they should not be confused. A page may conclude that a claim is weak as a stand-alone prediction while still acknowledging that it contributes to a broader Christian reading of Scripture.

That distinction is especially important for passages such as Psalm 22 and Genesis 3:15. Their importance in Christian theology is real, but much of that importance emerges from the larger scriptural network rather than from one isolated proof-text function.

## Role of the New Testament

The New Testament matters in this project, but it is not treated as a single undifferentiated kind of evidence. The project distinguishes at least three kinds of New Testament use.

### Quotation

Sometimes the New Testament explicitly quotes an Old Testament passage and applies it to Jesus. This is the clearest form of reuse, though it still does not settle whether the earlier text was originally predictive.

### Allusion

Sometimes the connection is looser. A Gospel, epistle, or speech may echo wording, imagery, or themes without formal quotation. These allusions can be theologically significant, but they require more interpretive care because they can be subtle and cumulative rather than decisive on their own.

### Narrative Shaping

Sometimes the strongest skeptical objection is that the later narrative may itself be told in conscious relation to Scripture. In those cases, the New Testament is not simply reporting a fulfillment from outside the text. It may also be presenting Jesus through an already scriptural lens. That does not make the connection false, but it does affect how criterion 6 should be weighed.

For that reason, New Testament use in this project is described as explicit quotation, close allusion, thematic use, or possible narrative shaping whenever that distinction matters. This helps keep the discussion analytical rather than impressionistic.

## Scripture Usage Policy

Scripture quotation is intentionally limited. Overview pages use selective quotation to orient the reader to the passage. Claim pages usually quote little or nothing and rely more heavily on references, paraphrase, and Bible Gateway links for full context.

The project uses the NRSVue for quoted text, keeps attribution explicit, and avoids reproducing long copyrighted blocks. When exact wording is uncertain, the page should summarize rather than imply false precision. The goal is not to reproduce entire chapters, but to show the reader the key lines needed to follow the argument responsibly.

## Limits of the Project

This project has clear limits. It does not attempt to settle every theological dispute about messianism, inspiration, or the authority of the New Testament. It also does not assume that one method can remove all ambiguity from poetic, symbolic, or theologically layered texts.

The project is likewise limited by genre and by evidence. Some passages are brief and underdetermined. Some are heavily debated in textual or historical terms. Some are central in later Christian doctrine without being strong under narrow predictive standards. A fair method should acknowledge those limits rather than hide them.

Finally, the project evaluates claims, not persons. It does not assume that Christians read every passage simplistically, and it does not assume that skeptical objections are always decisive. Its task is narrower: to describe the argument, identify the interpretive category, and show where the main evidential pressure points lie.

## How to Read This Project

Readers should approach each page by asking three questions.

1. What exactly is the claim being made?
2. What kind of fulfillment is being proposed: direct, typological, or canonical?
3. Which criteria actually carry the weight in this case?

That approach helps prevent category confusion. A passage may be strong as a theological pattern while weak as a direct prediction. Another may be textually early and messianically suggestive while still suffering from ambiguity or weak independence. The point of the project is not to force every page into the same verdict, but to explain clearly what sort of claim is on the table and how well it stands once its genre and context are taken seriously.
