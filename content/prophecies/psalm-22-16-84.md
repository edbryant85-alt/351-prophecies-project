# Psalm 22:16 — They pierced His hands and His feet

**Page Status:** Reviewed Analysis<br>
**Confidence Level:** Moderate<br>
**Last Updated:** 2026-04-29<br>

> NOTE: This page analyzes a specific prophecy claim. For a broader contextual overview, see [Psalm 22](./psalm-22.md).

## Claim
Christians often argue that Psalm 22:16 anticipates the piercing of Jesus' hands and feet. This is one of the most disputed claim pages in the entire Psalm 22 cluster because so much depends on the underlying Hebrew reading.

## Skeptic Criteria Evaluation
- Claimed failed criteria: 2, 3, 4, 5, 6
- Category clarification: From a historical-critical perspective, this claim is better classified as typological or canonical/theological rather than as a direct prediction. The strongest Christian case is that Psalm 22 supplies one of the densest Old Testament patterns behind the passion narratives. The strongest skeptical case is that the psalm remains lament poetry in context, not a direct messianic oracle.
- Brief skeptic summary:
  A common critical objection is that the claim summary, "They pierced His hands and His feet", extracts one vivid line from a lament and then matches it to Jesus retrospectively. This weakens criterion 2 because the psalm does not function as a prediction in context, criterion 5 because the imagery is poetic and often symbolic, and criterion 6 because the evangelists may have narrated Jesus' suffering with Psalm 22 consciously in view.

## Old Testament Passage
Psalm 22:16 describes the sufferer as surrounded by hostile enemies and then reaches the disputed line traditionally rendered either closer to "like a lion" or "they pierced." In context, the verse belongs to the psalm's bodily-distress and enemy-encirclement imagery.

## Old Testament Context
This line sits at the center of the psalm's most heavily debated section. Because Psalm 22 is poetry, the surrounding imagery is already compressed and metaphorical, which makes a disputed reading especially consequential.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers connect the verse with John 19:34-37 and 20:27, as well as with the broader crucifixion tradition. That connection can seem striking if the line is read in a "they pierced" direction, but it becomes much weaker if the Hebrew is taken closer to "like a lion" or another non-piercing reading.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "They pierced His hands and His feet" comes from lament poetry, not from a passage that presents itself as a future oracle about the Messiah. From a historical-critical perspective, Psalm 22 does not function in context as a direct prediction of Jesus, even where the later Gospel overlap is striking.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The main question is whether the New Testament use of this line is the most natural reading of Psalm 22 or a retrospective theological interpretation. The psalm originally gives voice to an afflicted worshiper in prayer; later Christian application may be profound without being identical to that original literary function.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Psalm 22 uses poetic compression, metaphor, and hyperbole. A single line can sound highly specific when isolated, but the surrounding psalm moves through lament, rescue, and praise rather than through a simple report of one future execution scene. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Because the evangelists knew Psalm 22 and sometimes quote or echo it directly, skeptics ask whether the passion narratives are merely reporting fulfillment or also presenting Jesus in conscious scriptural alignment. This is one of the clearest ways criterion 6 applies to these claims.

## Original Language Notes
The central issue is the debated wording behind translations such as "like a lion my hands and my feet" and "they pierced my hands and my feet." Major textual witnesses and ancient versions do not all point in exactly the same direction, which is why the verse remains contested. Because that textual decision shapes the entire apologetic use of the line, original-language analysis is indispensable here.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Ancient poetry often compresses syntax and imagery, which can make difficult lines harder to reconstruct with certainty. This is one reason the verse remains so debated in Jewish-Christian polemics and modern scholarship alike.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation has generally resisted the Christian "pierced" reading and treated the verse within the psalm's own poetic imagery. As a result, this line has often become a focal point of Jewish-Christian disagreement.

## Christian Interpretation
Many Christians see this as one of the strongest crucifixion correspondences in the Psalter. More careful Christian readings, however, usually acknowledge that the force of the claim depends heavily on textual and translational judgment.

## Textual Issues
This is the major textual crux in Psalm 22. Differences among the Masoretic Text, ancient versions such as the Septuagint, and later interpretive traditions have made verse 16 central to debates about whether Psalm 22 can support a strong crucifixion prediction claim. The uncertainty does not erase the verse's importance, but it does require much more caution than simplified apologetic appeals usually allow.

## My Conclusion
This is one of the more vivid Psalm 22 passion claims if the text is read as "they pierced," but it is also one of the most vulnerable because the wording is textually disputed. Under stricter criteria, it works better as part of a cumulative canonical reading than as a stand-alone direct prediction.
