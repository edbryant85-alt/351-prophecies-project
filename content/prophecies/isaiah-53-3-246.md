## Old Testament Passage
Isaiah 53:3 presents the servant as acquainted with sorrow and grief. The language contributes to the poem's portrayal of a figure whose suffering is sustained, visible, and socially consequential.

## Old Testament Context
Within the servant song, grief is not incidental; it is part of the servant's vocation and public misunderstanding. The poem invites readers to consider suffering as central to the servant's role before vindication arrives.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "Great sorrow and grief" is being extracted from a poetic servant song whose original referent is disputed. From a historical-critical perspective, Isaiah 53 does not clearly function in context as a direct prediction about Jesus of Nazareth, even if the Christian cumulative case remains powerful.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The key question is whether applying this line to Matt 26:37-38; Luke 19:41; Heb 4:15 is the most natural reading of the poem or a retrospective theological interpretation. Early Christians had strong reasons to reread the servant through Jesus' suffering and vindication, but that later christological use is not identical to the passage's original meaning.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Isaiah 53 is poetic, compressed, and open to collective, individual, and representative readings. Individual claims can look stronger when a vivid phrase is isolated, but the broader debate over the servant's identity, the exilic setting, and the poem's larger argument still remains in view. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Where the alleged fulfillment is drawn from the passion narratives, Acts, or apostolic theology, skeptics ask whether Jesus independently fulfills the line or whether his significance is being narrated through servant-song categories. That does not make the Christian reading meaningless, but it does weaken criterion 6 as evidence for stand-alone prediction.

## Original Language Notes
The Hebrew terms for sorrow and grief carry emotional and experiential weight, but they do not settle whether the suffering is ordinary, representative, or redemptive. Context does more interpretive work here than any single disputed word.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Exilic literature often links grief to national catastrophe, shame, and hope deferred. That background makes the verse intelligible within Isaiah's own world without requiring a direct forecast of Jesus.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation commonly hears this line as part of Israel's suffering or that of a righteous servant figure in history. It is therefore not usually isolated as a distinct prophecy of Jesus' emotional life.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians frequently see the verse as resonant with Jesus' sorrow in Gethsemane, his compassion, and his suffering throughout the passion. The verse is often read typologically or canonically even when not treated as a narrow prediction.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues are secondary here. The main dispute concerns how the emotional language should be situated in the larger servant portrait.
