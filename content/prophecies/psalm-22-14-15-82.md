## Old Testament Passage
Psalm 22:14-15 describes the sufferer as poured out, disjointed, weak, dried up, and near death. In context, these lines are the bodily climax of the lament before the closing plea for rescue.

## Old Testament Context
The psalm here moves from enemy pressure to inner physical collapse. The speaker experiences suffering in intensely embodied terms, yet the language remains poetic rather than clinical.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers connect this passage to the crucifixion generally and to Mark 15:34-37 in particular as part of the total scene of agony. The correspondence is strongest at the level of cumulative fit rather than precise quotation.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "Suffered agony on Calvary" comes from lament poetry, not from a passage that presents itself as a future oracle about the Messiah. From a historical-critical perspective, Psalm 22 does not function in context as a direct prediction of Jesus, even where the later Gospel overlap is striking.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The main question is whether the New Testament use of this line is the most natural reading of Psalm 22 or a retrospective theological interpretation. The psalm originally gives voice to an afflicted worshiper in prayer; later Christian application may be profound without being identical to that original literary function.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Psalm 22 uses poetic compression, metaphor, and hyperbole. A single line can sound highly specific when isolated, but the surrounding psalm moves through lament, rescue, and praise rather than through a simple report of one future execution scene. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Because the evangelists knew Psalm 22 and sometimes quote or echo it directly, skeptics ask whether the passion narratives are merely reporting fulfillment or also presenting Jesus in conscious scriptural alignment. This is one of the clearest ways criterion 6 applies to these claims.

## Original Language Notes
The bodily language is metaphor-rich: heart melting, strength drying up, tongue sticking, and being laid in the dust of death. These phrases intensify suffering but resist overly literal one-to-one mapping.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Ancient lament traditions frequently use bodily imagery to communicate mortal danger and social ruin. That background makes the resemblance to crucifixion suggestive, but not necessarily predictive in a strict sense.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish readings tend to hear these verses as the language of an extreme lament, whether Davidic or paradigmatic of righteous suffering. They are usually not read as a prophecy of Jesus' crucifixion.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians often see this section as one of the most powerful ways Psalm 22 resonates with the passion. The reading is usually cumulative, with the whole cluster of bodily images supporting the Christian case.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues are less central here than in verse 16. The main challenge is how literally the poetic language should be read.

## My Conclusion
This claim can contribute to a cumulative Christian case for Psalm 22 as a passion text, but under stricter criteria it is better classified as typological or canonical fulfillment than as a stand-alone direct prediction. The correspondence may be real, yet the skeptical case becomes stronger once lament genre, poetic ambiguity, and Gospel literary shaping are taken seriously.
