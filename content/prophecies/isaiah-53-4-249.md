## Old Testament Passage
Isaiah 53:4 moves from the servant's burden-bearing to the observers' false conclusion that he is stricken by God. The line is about perception as much as suffering: people see the servant as divinely judged, yet the poem redirects that conclusion.

## Old Testament Context
This verse is crucial because it distinguishes outward appearance from the deeper meaning of the servant's suffering. The poem suggests that the crowd misunderstands what the servant's affliction signifies.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers connect this line with mockery in Matthew 26:66 and 27:41-43, where Jesus' suffering is interpreted as evidence of divine rejection. The link is thematic and fits the broader passion narrative.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "Thought to be cursed by God" is being extracted from a poetic servant song whose original referent is disputed. From a historical-critical perspective, Isaiah 53 does not clearly function in context as a direct prediction about Jesus of Nazareth, even if the Christian cumulative case remains powerful.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The key question is whether applying this line to Matt 26:66; 27:41-43 is the most natural reading of the poem or a retrospective theological interpretation. Early Christians had strong reasons to reread the servant through Jesus' suffering and vindication, but that later christological use is not identical to the passage's original meaning.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Isaiah 53 is poetic, compressed, and open to collective, individual, and representative readings. Individual claims can look stronger when a vivid phrase is isolated, but the broader debate over the servant's identity, the exilic setting, and the poem's larger argument still remains in view. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Where the alleged fulfillment is drawn from the passion narratives, Acts, or apostolic theology, skeptics ask whether Jesus independently fulfills the line or whether his significance is being narrated through servant-song categories. That does not make the Christian reading meaningless, but it does weaken criterion 6 as evidence for stand-alone prediction.

## Original Language Notes
The language of being smitten or stricken by God is strong, but not especially obscure. The real issue is whether the verse is describing a historical misreading in Isaiah's context or predicting later interpretations of Jesus' death.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Ancient audiences often assumed suffering signaled divine punishment. Isaiah 53 pushes against that instinct, which makes the verse intelligible within its own theological world before any Christian application.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation typically reads the line as part of the servant's historical suffering and the mistaken judgments made about that suffering. It is not usually treated as a prophecy of reactions to Jesus at the crucifixion.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians often see the verse as fitting both the mockery directed at Jesus and the larger scandal of the cross. It can be read as direct prophecy, but many interpreters understand it more as a canonical pattern fulfilled in Jesus.

## Textual Issues
The main issues here are interpretive rather than textual. Manuscript differences do not substantially change the verse's role in the debate.
