## Skeptic Criteria Evaluation
Criteria 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 remain relevant. Skeptics argue that the verses are a closing plea for rescue in a lament psalm, and that linking them to Jesus' final words involves thematic association rather than close textual fulfillment.

## Old Testament Passage
Psalm 22:20-21 asks God to rescue the sufferer from deadly enemies. In the psalm, the lines are the urgent climax of the plea before the tone shifts toward vindication.

## Old Testament Context
These verses stand at the turning point of Psalm 22. The speaker commits his case entirely to God because no human rescue appears available.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers sometimes connect this to Luke 23:46 and Jesus' final committal to the Father. The relationship is thematic rather than verbal, and Luke's wording comes more directly from Psalm 31:5 than from Psalm 22:20-21.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
A skeptic will say this is a weak stand-alone claim because the closest verbal parallel lies elsewhere in the Psalms. Psalm 22:20-21 contributes to the passion atmosphere and to the broader logic of entrusting oneself to God, but it does not clearly predict Jesus' exact words or action.

## Original Language Notes
The rescue language is ordinary lament vocabulary. The focus is on deliverance from violent enemies, not on a distinctive lexical feature.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Appeal to God at the point of mortal danger is common in Israel's prayers. That makes the verses powerful devotionally, but less distinct as predictive evidence.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation usually reads these lines as the climactic plea of the psalmist for deliverance. They are not typically treated as a prophecy of Jesus' final entrustment to God.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians sometimes see these verses as fitting the posture of Jesus in the passion, especially his final dependence on the Father. The reading is usually broader and more thematic than with Psalm 22:1 or 18.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues are minor here. The interpretive weakness of the claim comes from the indirectness of the NT connection, not from manuscript instability.

## My Conclusion
This is a modest thematic claim rather than a strong predictive one. It can support the larger passion reading of Psalm 22, but it is not among the clearest correspondences and works best typologically.
