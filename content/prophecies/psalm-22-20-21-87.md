## Old Testament Passage
Psalm 22:20-21 asks God to rescue the sufferer from deadly enemies. In the psalm, the lines are the urgent climax of the plea before the tone shifts toward vindication.

## Old Testament Context
These verses stand at the turning point of Psalm 22. The speaker commits his case entirely to God because no human rescue appears available.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers sometimes connect this to Luke 23:46 and Jesus' final committal to the Father. The relationship is thematic rather than verbal, and Luke's wording comes more directly from Psalm 31:5 than from Psalm 22:20-21.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "He committed Himself to God" comes from lament poetry, not from a passage that presents itself as a future oracle about the Messiah. From a historical-critical perspective, Psalm 22 does not function in context as a direct prediction of Jesus, even where the later Gospel overlap is striking.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The main question is whether the New Testament use of this line is the most natural reading of Psalm 22 or a retrospective theological interpretation. The psalm originally gives voice to an afflicted worshiper in prayer; later Christian application may be profound without being identical to that original literary function.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Psalm 22 uses poetic compression, metaphor, and hyperbole. A single line can sound highly specific when isolated, but the surrounding psalm moves through lament, rescue, and praise rather than through a simple report of one future execution scene. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Because the evangelists knew Psalm 22 and sometimes quote or echo it directly, skeptics ask whether the passion narratives are merely reporting fulfillment or also presenting Jesus in conscious scriptural alignment. This is one of the clearest ways criterion 6 applies to these claims.

## Original Language Notes
The rescue language is ordinary lament vocabulary. The focus is on deliverance from violent enemies, not on a distinctive lexical feature.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Appeal to God at the point of mortal danger is common in Israel's prayers. That makes the verses powerful devotionally, but less distinct as predictive evidence.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation usually reads these lines as the climactic plea of the psalmist for deliverance. They are not typically treated as a prophecy of Jesus' final entrustment to God.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians sometimes see these verses as fitting the posture of Jesus in the passion, especially his final dependence on the Father. The reading is usually broader and more thematic than with Psalm 22:1 or 18.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues are minor here. The interpretive weakness of the claim comes from the indirectness of the NT connection, not from manuscript instability.

## My Conclusion
This claim can contribute to a cumulative Christian case for Psalm 22 as a passion text, but under stricter criteria it is better classified as typological or canonical fulfillment than as a stand-alone direct prediction. The correspondence may be real, yet the skeptical case becomes stronger once lament genre, poetic ambiguity, and Gospel literary shaping are taken seriously.
