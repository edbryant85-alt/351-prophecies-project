## Skeptic Criteria Evaluation
The skeptical pressure again falls on criteria 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6. Critics note that the language is highly metaphorical, with enemies described as bulls and lions, so any application to Jesus is necessarily interpretive.

## Old Testament Passage
Psalm 22:12-13 surrounds the sufferer with animal imagery representing overwhelming enemies. In context, the figures express menace and vulnerability rather than literal zoological description.

## Old Testament Context
These verses deepen the physical and social threat already established in the psalm. The speaker is not yet dead, but he is encircled by forces that seem ready to destroy him.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers sometimes connect this imagery to the opposition that culminates in Jesus' execution, including scenes such as John 19:6 where calls for death become explicit. The connection is general, metaphorical, and typological rather than verbal or predictive in a narrow sense.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
A skeptic will say the claim lacks specificity because the verses describe danger through animal metaphors that could fit many sufferers. The move from bulls and lions to a precise passion scene is possible as typology, but weak as stand-alone prediction. The highly poetic texture of the passage sets clear limits on literal one-to-one application.

## Original Language Notes
The animal imagery is conventional Hebrew poetic metaphor for strong and predatory enemies. The passage is vivid, but the metaphors make one-to-one predictive claims difficult to sustain.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Such imagery belongs naturally to lament traditions in which enemies are portrayed as beasts. That convention helps explain the power of the text while also limiting its predictive precision.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish readings generally treat the verses as metaphorical descriptions of enemies in a lament setting. They are not normally read as a prophecy of Jesus' death sentence.

## Christian Interpretation
Christian interpretation often hears these verses as part of the atmosphere of the passion, where Jesus is surrounded by hostile powers. The reading is usually typological or cumulative rather than based on a direct quotation.

## Textual Issues
The main issue here is poetic imagery, not textual uncertainty. The metaphors themselves are what complicate strict apologetic use.

## My Conclusion
This claim can contribute to the cumulative passion atmosphere of Psalm 22, but it is too metaphorical to stand strongly on its own. It is better treated as contextual or typological support than as a decisive proof text.
