## Old Testament Passage
Psalm 22:18 says that the speaker's garments are divided and lots are cast for his clothing. In the psalm, this image intensifies the humiliation of the sufferer by portraying enemies as already treating him as defeated spoil.

## Old Testament Context
This line appears at the climax of the shame-and-exposure sequence. The sufferer's enemies are not just mocking him; they are appropriating what belongs to him.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers connect the verse directly with John 19:23-24 and the parallel passion narratives. This is one of the clearest places where a Gospel writer explicitly invokes Psalm 22 in relation to the crucifixion.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
A skeptic will still say that the strength of the correspondence raises the independence problem. The verse may indeed be central to the Christian presentation of Jesus' death, but that does not automatically prove that Psalm 22 was originally a future prediction rather than a text deliberately reused in narrative construction.

## Original Language Notes
The garment and lot-casting language is straightforward. Unlike verse 16, the issue here is not major textual obscurity but the relationship between the psalm and the Gospel narration.

## Historical and Cultural Context
To divide a sufferer's garments is to treat him as helpless and defeated. The image naturally carries shame and plunder connotations within ancient warfare and punishment settings.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation usually reads the verse within the lament as another image of enemies humiliating the sufferer. It is not generally treated as a messianic prophecy about Jesus' clothing.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians often regard this as one of the strongest Psalm 22 correspondences in the passion narratives because the Gospel writers present the scene so explicitly. It can be read as direct prophecy or as canonical fulfillment, but either way it is central to Christian use of the psalm.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues are relatively minor here compared with other parts of Psalm 22. The core debate concerns independence and literary reuse.

## My Conclusion
This is one of the stronger claim-level links between Psalm 22 and the crucifixion because the narrative match is concrete and explicit. Even so, it still functions most responsibly within a cumulative framework that takes the independence question seriously.
