## Claim
Christians sometimes extend Daniel 9:26b into the claim that the Messiah dies not merely violently, but redemptively for others. In that reading, the death of the anointed one in verse 26 is interpreted in light of the reconciliation language earlier in the prophecy and then connected to Jesus' atoning death.

## Old Testament Passage
Daniel 9:26 describes an anointed one being cut off and then moves immediately to the devastation of the city and sanctuary. The local verse emphasizes loss, interruption, and judgment, not a direct explanation of how that death benefits others.

## Old Testament Context
The wider chapter is concerned with sin, exile, restoration, and the timetable by which these matters reach resolution. That larger frame explains why Christian interpreters connect the death in verse 26 to the atoning goals in verse 24. Even so, the verse itself remains compressed and does not spell out a doctrine of substitution.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
The dataset links this claim to Hebrews 2:9. The connection is conceptual rather than explicit: Daniel 9 is read as forecasting a messianic death within a redemptive timeline, and the New Testament supplies the fuller meaning of that death in relation to sin and salvation.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
Skeptics press criteria 2, 4, 5, and 6 especially strongly here. Criterion 5 is the most obvious problem, since Daniel 9:26 does not directly say that the anointed one dies for the sins of the world. Criterion 4 also weakens because the claim goes beyond the wording of the verse into theological inference. Criteria 2 and 6 enter because the Christian reading depends on integrating Daniel with later atonement categories rather than on a plainly self-contained prediction.

## Original Language Notes
The important term in the immediate verse remains `karat`, "cut off," which can indicate removal or death but does not itself explain purpose. The broader passage uses language about sin and reconciliation, but the step from that programmatic language to universal atonement is interpretive rather than lexical.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Apocalyptic texts often compress major theological claims into dense symbolic sequences. In Daniel 9, communal guilt, judgment, and restoration form the backdrop, but that does not mean each verse carries the full later doctrinal meaning Christians find in the chapter as a whole.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation has not generally taken this verse as a prediction that Jesus would die for the sins of the world. The line is more often read historically, in relation to the removal of an anointed figure within Israel's own story.

## Christian Interpretation
Christian interpretation often reads this claim through the whole of Daniel 9 rather than through verse 26 alone. Some readers see the death of the anointed one and the reconciliation goal of verse 24 as mutually explanatory, which makes the verse significant within a larger atonement reading centered on Jesus.

## Textual Issues
The main issue is interpretive rather than textual. The debate concerns how much theological content can responsibly be drawn from the phrase about the anointed one being cut off.

## My Conclusion
This is one of the weaker stand-alone Daniel 9 claims because it depends on combining several strands of interpretation rather than on the direct wording of the verse itself. It works better within a cumulative Christian reading of the whole passage than as an isolated proof of atoning death.
