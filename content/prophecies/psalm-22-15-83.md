## Claim
Christians often read Psalm 22:15 as anticipating Jesus' thirst on the cross. This is one of the more focused sub-claims because the imagery of dryness and the tongue plays naturally into the passion narrative.

## Old Testament Passage
Psalm 22:15 speaks of dried strength, a tongue sticking to the jaws, and being laid in the dust of death. In the psalm itself, the line combines thirst, weakness, and nearness to death.

## Old Testament Context
This verse is part of the bodily-collapse sequence in Psalm 22:14-15. Its emphasis is not merely on thirst as an isolated symptom, but on total depletion.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers commonly connect the line with John 19:28, where Jesus says, "I thirst." The fit is not an explicit quotation, but it is one of the more natural thematic links between Psalm 22 and the passion.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "He thirsted" comes from lament poetry, not from a passage that presents itself as a future oracle about the Messiah. From a historical-critical perspective, Psalm 22 does not function in context as a direct prediction of Jesus, even where the later Gospel overlap is striking.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The main question is whether the New Testament use of this line is the most natural reading of Psalm 22 or a retrospective theological interpretation. The psalm originally gives voice to an afflicted worshiper in prayer; later Christian application may be profound without being identical to that original literary function.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Psalm 22 uses poetic compression, metaphor, and hyperbole. A single line can sound highly specific when isolated, but the surrounding psalm moves through lament, rescue, and praise rather than through a simple report of one future execution scene. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Because the evangelists knew Psalm 22 and sometimes quote or echo it directly, skeptics ask whether the passion narratives are merely reporting fulfillment or also presenting Jesus in conscious scriptural alignment. This is one of the clearest ways criterion 6 applies to these claims.

## Original Language Notes
The dried-tongue imagery is vivid and not especially obscure. Its force is physical and poetic rather than textually uncertain.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Expressions of thirst and dryness are common in lament and distress language. That conventional background explains both why the verse resonates and why it is not uniquely specific.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation generally takes the verse as vivid suffering language within the lament. It is not commonly singled out as a messianic prediction.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians often treat this as one of the more persuasive minor correspondences between Psalm 22 and the passion. It is usually read as a cumulative but meaningful detail in the crucifixion narrative.

## Textual Issues
The text itself is fairly stable. The main issue is the ordinary-yet-evocative nature of the image.

## My Conclusion
This claim can contribute to a cumulative Christian case for Psalm 22 as a passion text, but under stricter criteria it is better classified as typological or canonical fulfillment than as a stand-alone direct prediction. The correspondence may be real, yet the skeptical case becomes stronger once lament genre, poetic ambiguity, and Gospel literary shaping are taken seriously.
