## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "Confined and persecuted" is being extracted from a poetic servant song whose original referent is disputed. From a historical-critical perspective, Isaiah 53 does not clearly function in context as a direct prediction about Jesus of Nazareth, even if the Christian cumulative case remains powerful.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The key question is whether applying this line to Matt 26:47-27:31 is the most natural reading of the poem or a retrospective theological interpretation. Early Christians had strong reasons to reread the servant through Jesus' suffering and vindication, but that later christological use is not identical to the passage's original meaning.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Isaiah 53 is poetic, compressed, and open to collective, individual, and representative readings. Individual claims can look stronger when a vivid phrase is isolated, but the broader debate over the servant's identity, the exilic setting, and the poem's larger argument still remains in view. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Where the alleged fulfillment is drawn from the passion narratives, Acts, or apostolic theology, skeptics ask whether Jesus independently fulfills the line or whether his significance is being narrated through servant-song categories. That does not make the Christian reading meaningless, but it does weaken criterion 6 as evidence for stand-alone prediction.

## Original Language Notes
The Hebrew in this verse is famously compressed, and translators differ over the precise nuance of the opening clause. That uncertainty reinforces the point that interpretation here is not simple or uncontested.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Exilic literature often portrays the righteous or the people of God as subject to oppressive power. That setting makes the verse historically intelligible without forcing a direct prediction of Jesus' arrest and trial.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish readings typically keep the verse within the servant poem's own horizon, whether collective or individual. It is therefore not usually isolated as a forecast of Jesus being arrested and abused.

## Christian Interpretation
Christian interpretation often sees the verse as fitting the coercive movement of Jesus through arrest, hearing, and sentence. Some treat this as direct fulfillment, while others regard it as a broader canonical pattern.

## Textual Issues
Both translation and interpretation matter here because the verse is compact and difficult. Even so, the debate is still more about sense than about whether the text existed before Christianity.

## My Conclusion
