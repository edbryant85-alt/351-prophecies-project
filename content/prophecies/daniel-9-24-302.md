## Old Testament Passage
Daniel 9:24 lists six outcomes to be accomplished within the seventy weeks. One of them is commonly translated along the lines of anointing or consecrating "the most holy," which places the phrase inside a larger restoration program involving sin, righteousness, Jerusalem, and sacred order.

## Old Testament Context
The context is Daniel's prayer over exile, sin, and the fate of the holy city. The answer concerns the future of the people, the city, and what is holy within Israel's restored life. That setting gives good reason to hear the phrase cultically or temple-related before turning it into a direct statement about a messianic person.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
The dataset links this claim to Luke 1:35, where Jesus is identified as holy from the beginning of his life. The connection is thematic rather than explicit: Daniel 9 is not quoted in Luke, and the fulfillment claim depends on reading the climax of holiness in the prophecy as centered in Jesus.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "He would be holy" is not what Daniel 9:24 most naturally says in the first place. From a historical-critical perspective, the phrase usually translated "most holy" is more plausibly cultic than personal, so the move from Daniel 9:24 to Jesus' holiness in Luke 1:35 is already a retrospective theological interpretation rather than a straightforward prediction.

### Dating / Postdiction Pressure
A critical objection is that the passage may reflect vaticinium ex eventu (prophecy written after the fact), which would weaken its predictive force. If the book's final form is read against the Antiochene crisis, the timetable may be explaining events already known to the writer rather than forecasting Jesus centuries in advance.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The key issue is whether Daniel's language about what is "most holy" belongs within the restoration of Jerusalem, temple order, and sacred space or whether it should be re-read later as a christological statement. The Christian reading may be coherent within a canonical framework, but it is not the most natural historical reading of the phrase.

### Chronology, Ambiguity, and Overfitting
The apparent precision depends on choosing a decree, deciding how to count the sevens, selecting an endpoint, and identifying the anointed one. This particular subclaim then adds another step by isolating one phrase from Daniel's sixfold program and treating it as a personal prediction. That flexibility allows multiple plausible fulfillments and makes retrofitting easier, which weakens criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Retrospective Harmonization
The New Testament does not supply one uncontested Daniel 9 fulfillment formula that resolves the chronology by itself. In practice, later Christian apologetic calculations do most of the harmonizing work, which weakens criterion 6.

## Original Language Notes
The crucial issue is the expression often rendered "most holy." In many biblical contexts, superlative holiness language refers to sanctuary space, sacred objects, or cultic status rather than to an individual. That lexical point is one reason many interpreters consider this claim more ambiguous than traditional prophecy lists suggest.

## Historical and Cultural Context
In biblical and Second Temple usage, holiness language commonly attaches to temple furnishings, sacred precincts, priestly activity, and consecrated space. Since Daniel 9 is concerned with Jerusalem and the holy city, a cultic reading fits the historical setting naturally.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation has generally not treated this line as a prediction of Jesus' holiness. It is more often read within the prophecy's concern for restoration, sanctity, and the future ordering of the holy people and city.

## Christian Interpretation
Some Christian readers take the phrase personally and see it fulfilled in Jesus as the Holy One. Others acknowledge that a cultic reading is possible but still argue that the prophecy reaches its fullest meaning in Christ, who embodies and surpasses temple holiness.

## Textual Issues
The main dispute is interpretive rather than text-critical. The question is not whether the phrase is present in Daniel 9, but whether it should be taken personally or cultically.

## My Conclusion
This claim is one of the weaker Daniel 9 subclaims under stricter criteria because the phrase is plausibly cultic rather than personal even before the chronology debate is settled. It works better as a Christian canonical or theological extension than as a stand-alone prediction that Jesus would be holy.
