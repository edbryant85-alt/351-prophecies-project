## Old Testament Passage
Psalm 22:31 ends with future generations being told that God has acted. In the psalm itself, the closing line seals the movement from suffering to praise by affirming completed divine deliverance.

## Old Testament Context
The final verse broadens the psalm's significance beyond the original sufferer. What God has done becomes something proclaimed to later generations.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers connect this line with John 19:30 and sometimes with Hebrews 10, treating the psalm's ending as resonant with the completion of Jesus' work. The connection is conceptual and typological rather than verbal.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim ""It is finished"" comes from lament poetry, not from a passage that presents itself as a future oracle about the Messiah. From a historical-critical perspective, Psalm 22 does not function in context as a direct prediction of Jesus, even where the later Gospel overlap is striking.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The main question is whether the New Testament use of this line is the most natural reading of Psalm 22 or a retrospective theological interpretation. The psalm originally gives voice to an afflicted worshiper in prayer; later Christian application may be profound without being identical to that original literary function.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Psalm 22 uses poetic compression, metaphor, and hyperbole. A single line can sound highly specific when isolated, but the surrounding psalm moves through lament, rescue, and praise rather than through a simple report of one future execution scene. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Because the evangelists knew Psalm 22 and sometimes quote or echo it directly, skeptics ask whether the passion narratives are merely reporting fulfillment or also presenting Jesus in conscious scriptural alignment. This is one of the clearest ways criterion 6 applies to these claims.

## Original Language Notes
The closing phrase is brief and interpretively important, but it does not supply an exact equivalent to the Gospel wording. Its force lies in completed action, not in a distinctive messianic formula.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Closing declarations of God's saving action are common in praise psalms. That conventional liturgical ending supports later theological reuse but limits strict predictive force.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation typically hears the final verse as the conclusion of a thanksgiving and proclamation sequence. It is not generally read as a prophecy of Jesus' final words from the cross.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians sometimes see the verse as an apt ending for a psalm that the passion narratives repeatedly echo, and therefore as fitting the completion of Jesus' saving work. The reading is usually typological or canonical rather than strictly predictive.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues are not central here. The weakness of the claim is conceptual distance rather than manuscript uncertainty.

## My Conclusion
This claim can contribute to a cumulative Christian case for Psalm 22 as a passion text, but under stricter criteria it is better classified as typological or canonical fulfillment than as a stand-alone direct prediction. The correspondence may be real, yet the skeptical case becomes stronger once lament genre, poetic ambiguity, and Gospel literary shaping are taken seriously.
