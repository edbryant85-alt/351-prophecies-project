## Claim
Christians often read Isaiah 53:9c as anticipating that Jesus would be without deceit. The claim stresses the servant's integrity, especially under suffering.

## Skeptic Criteria Evaluation
Criteria 2, 4, 5, and 6 are the main concerns. Skeptics argue that truthfulness and blameless speech are broad moral qualities, making the verse a weak stand-alone prediction unless it is read within the larger servant portrait.

## Old Testament Passage
Isaiah 53:9 adds that no deceit was found in the servant's mouth. In context, this detail reinforces the servant's innocence in both action and speech.

## Old Testament Context
The line belongs to the poem's insistence that the servant suffers unjustly. Together with the reference to nonviolence, it strengthens the impression that the servant is morally upright rather than guilty.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers commonly connect the verse to 1 Peter 2:22, where Jesus is explicitly described in language drawn from Isaiah 53. This gives the Christian use here a particularly clear New Testament anchor.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
A skeptic will still say that blameless speech is too general to function as a distinctive prophecy. The New Testament connection is strong, but it may show appropriation of Isaiah 53 rather than independent predictive verification.

## Original Language Notes
The term for deceit is ethically clear and not especially controversial lexically. The more important issue is whether the moral description should be read as directly messianic or more generally paradigmatic.

## Historical and Cultural Context
In biblical literature, righteous speech is often contrasted with violence and treachery. That background makes the line meaningful within Isaiah without requiring a later Jesus-specific referent.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation commonly understands the line as describing the servant's innocence and truthfulness within the poem's own horizon. It is not usually treated as a proof text for Jesus.

## Christian Interpretation
Christian interpretation often treats this as one of the more straightforward moral correspondences between the servant and Jesus, especially because of 1 Peter. Even so, some read it typologically rather than as a narrow prediction.

## Textual Issues
The textual tradition is not the main difficulty here. The argument turns on how much predictive weight a moral description can bear.

## My Conclusion
This is a relatively clear Christian application, but as a stand-alone claim it remains general. It is strongest when read in concert with the chapter's larger picture of the innocent servant.
