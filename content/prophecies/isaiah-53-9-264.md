## Old Testament Passage
Isaiah 53:9 adds that no deceit was found in the servant's mouth. In context, this detail reinforces the servant's innocence in both action and speech.

## Old Testament Context
The line belongs to the poem's insistence that the servant suffers unjustly. Together with the reference to nonviolence, it strengthens the impression that the servant is morally upright rather than guilty.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers commonly connect the verse to 1 Peter 2:22, where Jesus is explicitly described in language drawn from Isaiah 53. This gives the Christian use here a particularly clear New Testament anchor.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "No deceit in his mouth" is being extracted from a poetic servant song whose original referent is disputed. From a historical-critical perspective, Isaiah 53 does not clearly function in context as a direct prediction about Jesus of Nazareth, even if the Christian cumulative case remains powerful.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The key question is whether applying this line to 1 Pet 2:22 is the most natural reading of the poem or a retrospective theological interpretation. Early Christians had strong reasons to reread the servant through Jesus' suffering and vindication, but that later christological use is not identical to the passage's original meaning.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Isaiah 53 is poetic, compressed, and open to collective, individual, and representative readings. Individual claims can look stronger when a vivid phrase is isolated, but the broader debate over the servant's identity, the exilic setting, and the poem's larger argument still remains in view. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Where the alleged fulfillment is drawn from the passion narratives, Acts, or apostolic theology, skeptics ask whether Jesus independently fulfills the line or whether his significance is being narrated through servant-song categories. That does not make the Christian reading meaningless, but it does weaken criterion 6 as evidence for stand-alone prediction.

## Original Language Notes
The term for deceit is ethically clear and not especially controversial lexically. The more important issue is whether the moral description should be read as directly messianic or more generally paradigmatic.

## Historical and Cultural Context
In biblical literature, righteous speech is often contrasted with violence and treachery. That background makes the line meaningful within Isaiah without requiring a later Jesus-specific referent.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation commonly understands the line as describing the servant's innocence and truthfulness within the poem's own horizon. It is not usually treated as a proof text for Jesus.

## Christian Interpretation
Christian interpretation often treats this as one of the more straightforward moral correspondences between the servant and Jesus, especially because of 1 Peter. Even so, some read it typologically rather than as a narrow prediction.

## Textual Issues
The textual tradition is not the main difficulty here. The argument turns on how much predictive weight a moral description can bear.

## My Conclusion

The Christian case is strongest cumulatively; the skeptical case is strongest when original context, poetic ambiguity, and retrospective rereading are kept in view.
