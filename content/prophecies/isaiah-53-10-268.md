## Old Testament Passage
The latter part of Isaiah 53:10 says that the pleasure or purpose of the Lord will prosper in the servant's hand. In context, the line marks a reversal from humiliation to effective, divinely approved accomplishment.

## Old Testament Context
This verse makes clear that the servant song does not end in defeat. However the details are understood, the servant's suffering leads to successful fulfillment of God's purpose.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian interpreters often connect this with John 17:1-5 and the completion of Jesus' mission, though the correspondence is broader than a direct quotation. The emphasis is on successful fulfillment after suffering.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "He would prosper" is being extracted from a poetic servant song whose original referent is disputed. From a historical-critical perspective, Isaiah 53 does not clearly function in context as a direct prediction about Jesus of Nazareth, even if the Christian cumulative case remains powerful.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The key question is whether applying this line to John 17:1-5 is the most natural reading of the poem or a retrospective theological interpretation. Early Christians had strong reasons to reread the servant through Jesus' suffering and vindication, but that later christological use is not identical to the passage's original meaning.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Isaiah 53 is poetic, compressed, and open to collective, individual, and representative readings. Individual claims can look stronger when a vivid phrase is isolated, but the broader debate over the servant's identity, the exilic setting, and the poem's larger argument still remains in view. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Where the alleged fulfillment is drawn from the passion narratives, Acts, or apostolic theology, skeptics ask whether Jesus independently fulfills the line or whether his significance is being narrated through servant-song categories. That does not make the Christian reading meaningless, but it does weaken criterion 6 as evidence for stand-alone prediction.

## Original Language Notes
The prospering language is significant but not especially obscure. The question is whether the verse predicts a specific messianic outcome or simply states that God's purpose through the servant will not fail.

## Historical and Cultural Context
In exilic and restoration literature, divine purpose prevailing through suffering is a familiar theme. That makes the line meaningful in Isaiah's setting even apart from later Christian use.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation often reads this as the eventual success and vindication of the servant within Israel's story. It is not usually treated as a specific forecast of Jesus completing his mission.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians often take the verse as confirmation that the cross does not end in failure and that Jesus' work accomplishes God's purpose. Some see direct prophecy; others see a more general canonical fulfillment.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues are not central here. The debate is primarily about how specific the prospering language is meant to be.
