## Old Testament Passage
Isaiah 53:9 says the servant had done no violence. In context, the line helps explain the injustice of the servant's suffering and death.

## Old Testament Context
The servant song repeatedly invites readers to see a mismatch between the servant's character and the treatment he receives. Verse 9b sharpens that mismatch by stressing innocence in the face of condemnation.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian interpreters relate this claim to passages such as Luke 23:41 and John 18:38, where Jesus is presented as innocent. The fit is morally significant even though the New Testament is not always quoting the verse directly.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "Innocent and had done no violence" is being extracted from a poetic servant song whose original referent is disputed. From a historical-critical perspective, Isaiah 53 does not clearly function in context as a direct prediction about Jesus of Nazareth, even if the Christian cumulative case remains powerful.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The key question is whether applying this line to Luke 23:41; John 18:38 is the most natural reading of the poem or a retrospective theological interpretation. Early Christians had strong reasons to reread the servant through Jesus' suffering and vindication, but that later christological use is not identical to the passage's original meaning.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Isaiah 53 is poetic, compressed, and open to collective, individual, and representative readings. Individual claims can look stronger when a vivid phrase is isolated, but the broader debate over the servant's identity, the exilic setting, and the poem's larger argument still remains in view. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Where the alleged fulfillment is drawn from the passion narratives, Acts, or apostolic theology, skeptics ask whether Jesus independently fulfills the line or whether his significance is being narrated through servant-song categories. That does not make the Christian reading meaningless, but it does weaken criterion 6 as evidence for stand-alone prediction.

## Original Language Notes
The vocabulary of violence is clear enough, though its moral reach must be inferred from the context. The interpretive question is not what the word means, but who the servant is and how the line should be applied.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Ancient texts often distinguish between the righteous sufferer and the violent oppressor. That moral contrast is well at home in Isaiah's theological world.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish readings often understand this line as describing the innocence of the servant, whether Israel collectively or another righteous figure, without moving to Jesus as the intended referent.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians commonly see the servant's innocence as fitting Jesus' portrayal in the passion narratives. Some treat the verse as direct prophecy, while others see it as part of a cumulative righteous-sufferer pattern.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues are not decisive here. The debate is mainly over interpretation and application.
