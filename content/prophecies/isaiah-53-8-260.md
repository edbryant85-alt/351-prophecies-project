## Old Testament Passage
Isaiah 53:8 moves toward the statement that the servant is cut off from the land of the living. In context, the phrase indicates death or removal from life in a severe and final sense.

## Old Testament Context
This is a major turning point in the song because the servant's suffering is shown to culminate in death rather than merely hardship. The verse helps explain why Christians have long read the passage through the lens of the passion.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers connect the line to Jesus' execution, including references such as Matthew 27:35. The connection is strong at the level of broad narrative shape even if the New Testament does not always quote this exact clause explicitly.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "Killed" is being extracted from a poetic servant song whose original referent is disputed. From a historical-critical perspective, Isaiah 53 does not clearly function in context as a direct prediction about Jesus of Nazareth, even if the Christian cumulative case remains powerful.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The key question is whether applying this line to Matt 27:35 is the most natural reading of the poem or a retrospective theological interpretation. Early Christians had strong reasons to reread the servant through Jesus' suffering and vindication, but that later christological use is not identical to the passage's original meaning.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Isaiah 53 is poetic, compressed, and open to collective, individual, and representative readings. Individual claims can look stronger when a vivid phrase is isolated, but the broader debate over the servant's identity, the exilic setting, and the poem's larger argument still remains in view. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Where the alleged fulfillment is drawn from the passion narratives, Acts, or apostolic theology, skeptics ask whether Jesus independently fulfills the line or whether his significance is being narrated through servant-song categories. That does not make the Christian reading meaningless, but it does weaken criterion 6 as evidence for stand-alone prediction.

## Original Language Notes
The phrase about being cut off from the land of the living is theologically and literarily weighty. Even so, it does not specify method, date, or explicit messianic identity.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Death language in prophetic poetry often marks the extremity of suffering and injustice. In an exilic setting, that rhetoric can function powerfully without requiring a later one-to-one fulfillment.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation usually does not isolate the servant's death as a prophecy of Jesus' execution. The line is more often read within the broader suffering and vindication of Israel or a righteous figure.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians see the servant's death as one of the most important convergences with Jesus. Depending on the interpreter, this is read either as direct prophecy or as one major component in a typological and canonical pattern.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues do not dominate this claim. The main question is how much predictive specificity can rightly be drawn from the phrase.
