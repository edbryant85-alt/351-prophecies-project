## Claim
Christians often argue that Daniel 9:26 places the death of the anointed one before the destruction of Jerusalem and its sanctuary, which they then connect to Jesus' crucifixion before the temple's destruction in 70 CE. In apologetic use, this sequence is taken as an important confirmation that Daniel's timeline points to Jesus rather than to a later figure.

## Old Testament Passage
Daniel 9:26 moves from the cutting off of an anointed one to the destruction of the city and sanctuary by an invading people. The verse is therefore about sequence as much as content: the loss of the anointed figure is followed by urban and cultic devastation.

## Old Testament Context
The prophecy addresses Daniel's concern for Jerusalem by revealing a prolonged and troubled future for the holy city. In that setting, the destruction language functions as part of a larger pattern of desecration, crisis, and judgment. Whether this points forward to 70 CE or to an earlier historical catastrophe is one of the central interpretive questions in the chapter.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
The dataset links this claim to Matthew 27:50-51 to mark Jesus' death, while the larger fulfillment argument depends on pairing that death with the later Roman destruction of Jerusalem. Christian interpreters therefore treat the verse as a sequence claim: Messiah dies, then the sanctuary falls.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
Skeptics usually raise criteria 2, 4, 5, and 6. Criterion 4 is disputed because the sequence only becomes persuasive after multiple identifications are chosen: the anointed one must be Jesus, the destruction must be 70 CE, and the chronology must align accordingly. Criterion 5 remains an issue because the verse does not name Rome or the Second Temple's final destruction explicitly. Criteria 2 and 6 matter because many scholars read the verse in relation to earlier historical turmoil and see the Christian application as a later reconfiguration around Jesus and 70 CE.

## Original Language Notes
The key expressions involve the anointed one being `karat`, "cut off," and the subsequent destruction of city and sanctuary. The language is forceful but still compressed, which leaves room for disagreement over the exact historical referents.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Jewish apocalyptic writing often interprets catastrophe through patterned sequences of sin, judgment, desecration, and hoped-for restoration. Because Jerusalem's destruction was a recurring trauma in Jewish memory, this kind of language can be read in relation to more than one crisis, which helps explain why the verse remains contested.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation has not generally treated this verse as a forecast of Jesus dying before the Roman destruction of the temple. More often, it has been read in relation to crises internal to Jewish history and to the fate of Jerusalem within those settings.

## Christian Interpretation
Christian interpreters often present this as a powerful sequence claim: Messiah dies, then the city and sanctuary are destroyed. Many regard that order as a major apologetic strength of Daniel 9, even though they differ on the details of the broader timeline.

## Textual Issues
The main difficulty is interpretive rather than textual. The dispute concerns historical identification and chronology more than manuscript instability.
