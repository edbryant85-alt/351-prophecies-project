## Claim
Christians often read Daniel 7:13-14 as a major messianic text because the "one like a son of man" receives dominion, glory, kingship, and an everlasting kingdom from the Ancient of Days. The strongest Christian case is that Jesus and the New Testament repeatedly draw on Danielic Son of Man imagery to explain his authority, exaltation, return, and universal reign.

## Skeptic Criteria Evaluation
Skeptics commonly argue that Daniel 7 fails criteria 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 when treated as a direct prediction of Jesus. The most important dispute is criterion 2, predictive intent: in context, the vision contrasts beastly empires with God's judgment and the vindication of the holy ones. Criteria 4 and 5 are also contested because the "one like a son of man" may be symbolic, corporate, angelic, royal, messianic, or some combination. Criterion 6 matters because the New Testament's use of Son of Man language is deliberate theological interpretation rather than a simple one-verse fulfillment formula.

## Old Testament Passage
Daniel 7 presents a symbolic vision of four beasts, a heavenly court, the Ancient of Days, judgment against arrogant empire, and a humanlike figure who comes with the clouds of heaven and receives dominion. The chapter later interprets the kingdom as belonging to the holy ones of the Most High, which is one reason interpreters debate whether the humanlike figure represents an individual, the faithful people, an angelic representative, or a royal-messianic figure.

As an overview page, this entry covers the Daniel 7 cluster rather than only one phrase. Claim 297 focuses on heavenly ascent or coming with the clouds, claim 298 on exaltation, and claim 299 on everlasting dominion.

## Old Testament Context
Daniel 7 belongs to apocalyptic literature, where symbolic visions interpret imperial violence and divine sovereignty. Many historical-critical scholars connect the chapter to the second-century BCE crisis under Antiochus IV, reading the beasts as oppressive kingdoms and the vindication scene as hope for the faithful under persecution. More traditional readings often treat the vision as a long-range prophecy of successive empires and the final triumph of God's kingdom.

Both approaches matter for the project. The passage is clearly prior to the New Testament, but its original referent and genre are debated. It does not read like a simple biographical prediction; it reads like an apocalyptic courtroom vision about kingdom, judgment, and vindication.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian interpretation connects Daniel 7 to Jesus in several ways. Jesus' preferred title "Son of Man" often evokes Danielic imagery, especially in sayings about authority, suffering, vindication, and coming with the clouds. Acts 1:9-11 is associated with heavenly ascent, Ephesians 1:20-22 with exaltation above powers, and Luke 1:31-33 with an everlasting royal reign.

The New Testament does not use Daniel 7 only as a prediction checklist. It treats Daniel's vision as a scriptural framework for Jesus' identity: he is the representative human one, the vindicated sufferer, and the king whose dominion belongs with God's kingdom.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### C2 Predictive Intent
The core skeptical objection is that Daniel 7 first addresses oppressive empires and the vindication of God's people. The "one like a son of man" is embedded in symbolic apocalyptic imagery, not introduced as a direct forecast of Jesus of Nazareth.

### C3 Non-Mundane
The imagery is highly exalted and unusual, so this criterion is not as weak as in many broad royal texts. Still, skeptics argue that apocalyptic visions often use cosmic imagery to speak about historical crisis, so the impressive imagery alone does not prove a specific messianic prediction.

### C4 Specific and Verifiable
The passage points to dominion, judgment, and universal kingship, but it does not name Jesus, describe the crucifixion, or identify a clear first-century event. The Christian fit becomes strongest when read through the New Testament's broader use of Danielic language.

### C5 Clarity
Interpretation turns on the identity of the humanlike figure. Is he an individual messiah, an angelic representative, faithful Israel, the saints collectively, or a figure who embodies the saints? The text itself leaves enough room for serious disagreement.

### C6 Independence
The New Testament writers and early Christians knew Danielic language and used it intentionally. Skeptics therefore ask whether Daniel 7 independently predicts Jesus or whether Jesus' significance is being articulated through Daniel's apocalyptic symbols.

## Original Language Notes
Daniel 7 is written in Aramaic. The phrase often rendered "one like a son of man" is idiomatic for a humanlike figure, especially in contrast to the beastly figures earlier in the vision. The cloud imagery is also important because coming with clouds is often associated with divine authority or heavenly status, which is one reason the passage became so important in later Jewish and Christian interpretation.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Apocalyptic literature often represents political empires as monsters and God's judgment as a heavenly court scene. Daniel 7 fits that pattern: violent kingdoms dominate for a time, but ultimate authority belongs to God and is given to the humanlike figure and the holy ones. This setting helps explain why the passage can be read both as resistance literature in a crisis and as a broader theological vision of God's final rule.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation is diverse. Some readings emphasize the "holy ones" collectively and understand the humanlike figure as representing faithful Israel or the saints. Others allow a more individual, angelic, or messianic interpretation. What Jewish readings generally resist is the claim that the passage transparently identifies Jesus; the text remains rooted in Daniel's apocalyptic vision of empire, judgment, and the vindication of God's people.

## Christian Interpretation
Christian interpretation has long treated Daniel 7 as one of the most important backgrounds for Jesus' identity and exaltation. The title "Son of Man," the imagery of clouds, and the gift of universal dominion all became central to Christian claims about Jesus' authority, resurrection vindication, ascension, and return. More careful Christian readings often frame this as canonical fulfillment rather than as a simple direct prediction detached from Daniel's original context.

## Textual Issues
The main issues are interpretive rather than textual. The wording of the humanlike figure and dominion scene is stable enough for the debate, but the meaning of the symbols remains contested. The relationship between Daniel 7:13-14 and Daniel 7:18, 22, and 27 is especially important because those later verses connect the kingdom to the holy ones.

## My Conclusion
Daniel 7 is a strong Christian messianic text at the level of canonical theology because the New Testament repeatedly uses its imagery to describe Jesus' authority and vindication. Under strict predictive criteria, however, it is harder to treat as a stand-alone direct forecast because the original vision is symbolic, corporate in places, and rooted in apocalyptic resistance to empire. It works best as a major typological or canonical fulfillment text rather than as a simple one-to-one prediction.
