## Old Testament Passage
Isaiah 53:12 continues by saying that the servant bore the sin of many. In context, this line summarizes the meaning of the servant's suffering after the poem has already narrated death and vindication.

## Old Testament Context
The chapter closes by returning to the theme of vicarious burden-bearing. This repetition is important because it shows that the servant's suffering is not accidental ornament but central to the poem's theology.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers often connect this claim with 1 Peter 2:24 and similar New Testament statements about Jesus bearing sins. The Christian use here is direct in theme even when not presented as a formal quotation.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "Sin-bearer for all mankind" is being extracted from a poetic servant song whose original referent is disputed. From a historical-critical perspective, Isaiah 53 does not clearly function in context as a direct prediction about Jesus of Nazareth, even if the Christian cumulative case remains powerful.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The key question is whether applying this line to 1 Pet 2:24 is the most natural reading of the poem or a retrospective theological interpretation. Early Christians had strong reasons to reread the servant through Jesus' suffering and vindication, but that later christological use is not identical to the passage's original meaning.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Isaiah 53 is poetic, compressed, and open to collective, individual, and representative readings. Individual claims can look stronger when a vivid phrase is isolated, but the broader debate over the servant's identity, the exilic setting, and the poem's larger argument still remains in view. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Where the alleged fulfillment is drawn from the passion narratives, Acts, or apostolic theology, skeptics ask whether Jesus independently fulfills the line or whether his significance is being narrated through servant-song categories. That does not make the Christian reading meaningless, but it does weaken criterion 6 as evidence for stand-alone prediction.

## Original Language Notes
The verb for bearing sin is important and repeatedly discussed in relation to sacrificial and representative categories. Even so, the lexical field does not eliminate debate over whether the language is substitutionary, symbolic, or corporate.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Israel's scriptures often connect guilt, mediation, and restoration. That background makes the line intelligible in Isaiah's own theological setting and also helps explain why Christians found it so compelling.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation typically does not read this line as a prophecy of Jesus bearing the sins of humanity. It is more often understood within Israel's suffering, the righteous remnant, or another servant figure.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians often regard this line as one of the clearest summaries of the servant's atoning role and apply it directly to Jesus. Some call it direct prophecy; others describe it as canonical fulfillment.

## Textual Issues
Textual questions are not decisive here. The main debate is over meaning and referent, not over manuscript instability.

## My Conclusion
This claim is still meaningful within the larger Christian reading of the servant song, but under stricter historical criteria it is better classified as typological or canonical fulfillment than as a stand-alone direct prediction. The Christian case is strongest cumulatively; the skeptical case is strongest when original context, poetic ambiguity, and retrospective rereading are kept in view.
