## Skeptic Criteria Evaluation
Criteria 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 remain central because this line again frames the servant as bearing sin. Skeptics argue that repeated sin-bearing language in Isaiah 53 can support representative or theological readings without requiring a direct prediction of Jesus.

## Old Testament Passage
Isaiah 53:12 continues by saying that the servant bore the sin of many. In context, this line summarizes the meaning of the servant's suffering after the poem has already narrated death and vindication.

## Old Testament Context
The chapter closes by returning to the theme of vicarious burden-bearing. This repetition is important because it shows that the servant's suffering is not accidental ornament but central to the poem's theology.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers often connect this claim with 1 Peter 2:24 and similar New Testament statements about Jesus bearing sins. The Christian use here is direct in theme even when not presented as a formal quotation.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
A skeptic will say the line still leaves open who the servant is and how exactly sin-bearing should be understood. The Christian claim is coherent, but it remains part of a larger interpretive framework rather than a stand-alone predictive demonstration.

## Original Language Notes
The verb for bearing sin is important and repeatedly discussed in relation to sacrificial and representative categories. Even so, the lexical field does not eliminate debate over whether the language is substitutionary, symbolic, or corporate.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Israel's scriptures often connect guilt, mediation, and restoration. That background makes the line intelligible in Isaiah's own theological setting and also helps explain why Christians found it so compelling.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation typically does not read this line as a prophecy of Jesus bearing the sins of humanity. It is more often understood within Israel's suffering, the righteous remnant, or another servant figure.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians often regard this line as one of the clearest summaries of the servant's atoning role and apply it directly to Jesus. Some call it direct prophecy; others describe it as canonical fulfillment.

## Textual Issues
Textual questions are not decisive here. The main debate is over meaning and referent, not over manuscript instability.

## My Conclusion
This sub-claim is strong within the chapter's cumulative Christian reading because it restates the servant's burden-bearing role so plainly. It remains weaker when isolated from the broader theology of Isaiah 53.
