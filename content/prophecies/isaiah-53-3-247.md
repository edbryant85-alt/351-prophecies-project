## Old Testament Passage
The verse describes the servant as one from whom people hide their faces or turn away. In context, that image heightens the poem's theme of shame and failed recognition.

## Old Testament Context
Isaiah 53 portrays the servant as not merely suffering but also being treated as socially contaminating or embarrassing. The line reinforces the contrast between human dismissal and later divine vindication.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers connect this idea to scenes such as the disciples fleeing in Mark 14:50-52 and to the broader reluctance of many to be associated with Jesus in his humiliation. The connection is interpretive rather than tightly lexical.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "Men hide from being associated with Him" is being extracted from a poetic servant song whose original referent is disputed. From a historical-critical perspective, Isaiah 53 does not clearly function in context as a direct prediction about Jesus of Nazareth, even if the Christian cumulative case remains powerful.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The key question is whether applying this line to Mark 14:50-52 is the most natural reading of the poem or a retrospective theological interpretation. Early Christians had strong reasons to reread the servant through Jesus' suffering and vindication, but that later christological use is not identical to the passage's original meaning.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Isaiah 53 is poetic, compressed, and open to collective, individual, and representative readings. Individual claims can look stronger when a vivid phrase is isolated, but the broader debate over the servant's identity, the exilic setting, and the poem's larger argument still remains in view. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Where the alleged fulfillment is drawn from the passion narratives, Acts, or apostolic theology, skeptics ask whether Jesus independently fulfills the line or whether his significance is being narrated through servant-song categories. That does not make the Christian reading meaningless, but it does weaken criterion 6 as evidence for stand-alone prediction.

## Original Language Notes
The image of hiding the face is a conventional way of expressing revulsion, shame, or refusal. The phrase is powerful, but the debate lies more in literary application than in disputed translation.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Honor and shame dynamics are central in the ancient world. A figure publicly marked by suffering could easily become one from whom others distance themselves, which gives the line a clear historical sense within Isaiah's own setting.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish readers often take the verse as describing how the servant, whether Israel or another righteous figure, is treated with contempt by others. The line is not usually read as a unique prophecy of Jesus' abandonment.

## Christian Interpretation
Christian interpretation sees the verse as fitting the disciples' failure, public shame, and general distancing from Jesus in his suffering. It is often treated as a cumulative detail rather than a decisive proof text by itself.

## Textual Issues
The textual tradition is not the main issue here. Interpretation turns on imagery and application rather than on major manuscript divergence.
