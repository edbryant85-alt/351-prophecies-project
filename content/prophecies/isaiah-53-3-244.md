## Old Testament Passage
Isaiah 53:3 opens by describing the servant as despised and treated as one from whom people turn away. The line deepens the poem's emphasis on public rejection rather than public acclaim.

## Old Testament Context
This verse belongs to the central humiliation section of Isaiah 53. Whether the servant is read as Israel, a righteous remnant, or an individual figure, the poem stresses that the servant's role is not recognized in ordinary social terms.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christians connect the verse to episodes in which Jesus is scorned, opposed, or dismissed, including scenes of rejection in Luke 4:28-29 and elsewhere. The New Testament link is usually thematic rather than a formal quotation of Isaiah 53:3a.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "Despised" is being extracted from a poetic servant song whose original referent is disputed. From a historical-critical perspective, Isaiah 53 does not clearly function in context as a direct prediction about Jesus of Nazareth, even if the Christian cumulative case remains powerful.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The key question is whether applying this line to Luke 4:28-29 is the most natural reading of the poem or a retrospective theological interpretation. Early Christians had strong reasons to reread the servant through Jesus' suffering and vindication, but that later christological use is not identical to the passage's original meaning.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Isaiah 53 is poetic, compressed, and open to collective, individual, and representative readings. Individual claims can look stronger when a vivid phrase is isolated, but the broader debate over the servant's identity, the exilic setting, and the poem's larger argument still remains in view. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Where the alleged fulfillment is drawn from the passion narratives, Acts, or apostolic theology, skeptics ask whether Jesus independently fulfills the line or whether his significance is being narrated through servant-song categories. That does not make the Christian reading meaningless, but it does weaken criterion 6 as evidence for stand-alone prediction.

## Original Language Notes
The key term usually rendered "despised" conveys contempt or low valuation. The lexical point is not especially disputed; the debate is over who is being described and whether contempt in this poem should be read messianically.

## Historical and Cultural Context
In exilic and post-exilic rhetoric, shame and contempt are powerful social realities. The verse draws on that world of dishonor and public reversal rather than on a narrowly predictive timetable.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation often reads this humiliation language in relation to Israel's suffering, the suffering of the righteous, or another non-Jesus referent within Isaiah's horizon. The verse is therefore usually not isolated as a prophecy of Jesus.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians commonly read the servant's contempt and dishonor as fitting Jesus' treatment in his ministry and passion. Some see direct prophecy here, while others treat it as a pattern Jesus brings to its fullest expression.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues are limited in this line. The disagreement is primarily interpretive, not based on a major variant between the Masoretic Text and other witnesses.

## My Conclusion
This claim is still meaningful within the larger Christian reading of the servant song, but under stricter historical criteria it is better classified as typological or canonical fulfillment than as a stand-alone direct prediction. The Christian case is strongest cumulatively; the skeptical case is strongest when original context, poetic ambiguity, and retrospective rereading are kept in view.
