## Old Testament Passage
Psalm 22:2 continues the lament by describing unanswered prayer through day and night. The emphasis is on relentless distress and divine silence rather than on any visible cosmic sign.

## Old Testament Context
In Psalm 22, this line intensifies the speaker's anguish after the opening cry of abandonment. It functions as part of the psalm's complaint structure rather than as a discrete image of a later event.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers sometimes connect the verse with Matthew 27:45 and the darkness during the crucifixion, but the relationship is highly inferential. The New Testament does not quote Psalm 22:2 in connection with that scene, and the proposed link is thematic rather than predictive.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "Darkness upon Calvary for three hours" comes from lament poetry, not from a passage that presents itself as a future oracle about the Messiah. From a historical-critical perspective, Psalm 22 does not function in context as a direct prediction of Jesus, even where the later Gospel overlap is striking.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The main question is whether the New Testament use of this line is the most natural reading of Psalm 22 or a retrospective theological interpretation. The psalm originally gives voice to an afflicted worshiper in prayer; later Christian application may be profound without being identical to that original literary function.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Psalm 22 uses poetic compression, metaphor, and hyperbole. A single line can sound highly specific when isolated, but the surrounding psalm moves through lament, rescue, and praise rather than through a simple report of one future execution scene. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Because the evangelists knew Psalm 22 and sometimes quote or echo it directly, skeptics ask whether the passion narratives are merely reporting fulfillment or also presenting Jesus in conscious scriptural alignment. This is one of the clearest ways criterion 6 applies to these claims.

## Original Language Notes
The verse uses ordinary lament language about crying and lack of rest. There is no clear lexical hook here that naturally points to a darkness motif.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Day-and-night complaint is common in biblical prayer language. That background makes the verse meaningful in Israel's worship without suggesting a precise future sign.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish readings typically understand this line as part of the intensity of the psalmist's distress. It is not usually connected to a future messianic event, much less to a crucifixion setting.

## Christian Interpretation
Most Christian interpreters give this verse less direct attention than Psalm 22:1, 7-8, or 18. When it is connected to Jesus, it is usually through broad resonance rather than direct fulfillment.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues are minimal here. The weakness of the claim is interpretive, not textual.
