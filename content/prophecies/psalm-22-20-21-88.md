## Skeptic Criteria Evaluation
Criteria 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 all weigh heavily here. Skeptics argue that the psalm speaks of enemies through poetic animal imagery, not of Satan in an explicit or clearly predictive sense.

## Old Testament Passage
Psalm 22:20-21 asks for deliverance from the sword, dogs, lions, and wild oxen. In the psalm, these figures represent extreme danger and hostile attack.

## Old Testament Context
The imagery here belongs to the psalm's closing plea for rescue. The animals intensify the menace faced by the sufferer, but the psalm does not itself identify them as demonic beings.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers sometimes connect this with Hebrews 2:14 and with broader theological ideas about Christ's conflict with evil. The link is conceptual, doctrinal, and typological rather than textual.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
A skeptic will say this is one of the weakest stand-alone claim pages because the proposed fulfillment depends on reading symbolic enemies as Satanic power. That move may be meaningful within later Christian theology, but it is difficult to defend as a clear prediction from the psalm itself. The psalm's language remains metaphorical and does not explicitly shift from human enemies to cosmic evil.

## Original Language Notes
The animal language is metaphorical and forceful, but not especially obscure. The issue is whether later theological interpretation may legitimately extend the imagery to cosmic evil.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Animal metaphors for enemies are common in lament poetry across the Hebrew Bible. That conventional background makes a strictly satanic reading far from obvious in the psalm's original setting.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish readings generally take the verse as poetic enemy imagery within a lament, not as a prophecy of Satan bruising a redeemer. The Christian move here is typically seen as far removed from the psalm's original context.

## Christian Interpretation
Some Christian interpreters place this verse within a larger biblical pattern of Christ confronting evil powers. That reading is usually typological or theological rather than claimable as direct prediction.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues are not central here. The main challenge is hermeneutical distance between the psalm and the later doctrinal claim.
