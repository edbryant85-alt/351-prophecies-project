## Old Testament Passage
Psalm 22:20-21 asks for deliverance from the sword, dogs, lions, and wild oxen. In the psalm, these figures represent extreme danger and hostile attack.

## Old Testament Context
The imagery here belongs to the psalm's closing plea for rescue. The animals intensify the menace faced by the sufferer, but the psalm does not itself identify them as demonic beings.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers sometimes connect this with Hebrews 2:14 and with broader theological ideas about Christ's conflict with evil. The link is conceptual, doctrinal, and typological rather than textual.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "Satanic power bruising the Redeemer's heel" comes from lament poetry, not from a passage that presents itself as a future oracle about the Messiah. From a historical-critical perspective, Psalm 22 does not function in context as a direct prediction of Jesus, even where the later Gospel overlap is striking.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The main question is whether the New Testament use of this line is the most natural reading of Psalm 22 or a retrospective theological interpretation. The psalm originally gives voice to an afflicted worshiper in prayer; later Christian application may be profound without being identical to that original literary function.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Psalm 22 uses poetic compression, metaphor, and hyperbole. A single line can sound highly specific when isolated, but the surrounding psalm moves through lament, rescue, and praise rather than through a simple report of one future execution scene. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Because the evangelists knew Psalm 22 and sometimes quote or echo it directly, skeptics ask whether the passion narratives are merely reporting fulfillment or also presenting Jesus in conscious scriptural alignment. This is one of the clearest ways criterion 6 applies to these claims.

## Original Language Notes
The animal language is metaphorical and forceful, but not especially obscure. The issue is whether later theological interpretation may legitimately extend the imagery to cosmic evil.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Animal metaphors for enemies are common in lament poetry across the Hebrew Bible. That conventional background makes a strictly satanic reading far from obvious in the psalm's original setting.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish readings generally take the verse as poetic enemy imagery within a lament, not as a prophecy of Satan bruising a redeemer. The Christian move here is typically seen as far removed from the psalm's original context.

## Christian Interpretation
Some Christian interpreters place this verse within a larger biblical pattern of Christ confronting evil powers. That reading is usually typological or theological rather than claimable as direct prediction.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues are not central here. The main challenge is hermeneutical distance between the psalm and the later doctrinal claim.

## My Conclusion
This claim can contribute to a cumulative Christian case for Psalm 22 as a passion text, but under stricter criteria it is better classified as typological or canonical fulfillment than as a stand-alone direct prediction. The correspondence may be real, yet the skeptical case becomes stronger once lament genre, poetic ambiguity, and Gospel literary shaping are taken seriously.
