## Old Testament Passage
Psalm 22:14 uses bodily imagery of being poured out like water and of bones being out of joint. In the psalm, the speaker is describing physical breakdown and extreme vulnerability.

## Old Testament Context
This verse belongs to the psalm's most intense bodily-distress section. The language is figurative but vivid, moving the lament from social shame into felt physical disintegration.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers sometimes connect the line with John 19:34, but the connection is indirect. John does not quote Psalm 22:14 here, and the proposed fulfillment depends on extending the psalm's bodily imagery beyond its immediate metaphorical force.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "His blood poured out when they pierced His side" comes from lament poetry, not from a passage that presents itself as a future oracle about the Messiah. From a historical-critical perspective, Psalm 22 does not function in context as a direct prediction of Jesus, even where the later Gospel overlap is striking.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The main question is whether the New Testament use of this line is the most natural reading of Psalm 22 or a retrospective theological interpretation. The psalm originally gives voice to an afflicted worshiper in prayer; later Christian application may be profound without being identical to that original literary function.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Psalm 22 uses poetic compression, metaphor, and hyperbole. A single line can sound highly specific when isolated, but the surrounding psalm moves through lament, rescue, and praise rather than through a simple report of one future execution scene. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Because the evangelists knew Psalm 22 and sometimes quote or echo it directly, skeptics ask whether the passion narratives are merely reporting fulfillment or also presenting Jesus in conscious scriptural alignment. This is one of the clearest ways criterion 6 applies to these claims.

## Original Language Notes
The phrase "poured out like water" is conventional poetic language for depletion and collapse. Its imagery is powerful, but it does not naturally identify one specific injury mechanism.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Hebrew lament often uses bodily metaphors to dramatize distress. That convention explains the verse well within the psalm itself without requiring a later crucifixion detail.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation usually reads the verse as metaphorical language for extreme suffering. It is not typically connected to a distinct messianic wound or to Jesus' side being pierced.

## Christian Interpretation
Some Christian readings treat the verse as anticipatory of the crucifixion's bodily violence, including John's account of the pierced side. Others use it more cautiously as part of the larger passion atmosphere of the psalm.

## Textual Issues
The text is reasonably stable; the issue is the figurative force of the imagery. The claim rises or falls more on interpretation than on textual criticism.

## My Conclusion
This claim can contribute to a cumulative Christian case for Psalm 22 as a passion text, but under stricter criteria it is better classified as typological or canonical fulfillment than as a stand-alone direct prediction. The correspondence may be real, yet the skeptical case becomes stronger once lament genre, poetic ambiguity, and Gospel literary shaping are taken seriously.
