## Old Testament Passage
After speaking of the servant's offering, Isaiah 53:10 goes on to describe the servant as seeing offspring and prolonging days. In context, this creates a striking tension with the earlier statements about death.

## Old Testament Context
This is one of the verses that pushes the servant song beyond simple tragedy. The servant is not left in death; some form of continued life, vindication, or enduring effect follows.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers commonly connect this claim with Romans 6:9 and the broader New Testament witness to Jesus' resurrection. The linkage is inferential rather than explicit: the verse is taken to imply post-death continuation that Christians identify with resurrection life.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "Resurrected and live forever" is being extracted from a poetic servant song whose original referent is disputed. From a historical-critical perspective, Isaiah 53 does not clearly function in context as a direct prediction about Jesus of Nazareth, even if the Christian cumulative case remains powerful.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The key question is whether applying this line to Rom 6:9 is the most natural reading of the poem or a retrospective theological interpretation. Early Christians had strong reasons to reread the servant through Jesus' suffering and vindication, but that later christological use is not identical to the passage's original meaning.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Isaiah 53 is poetic, compressed, and open to collective, individual, and representative readings. Individual claims can look stronger when a vivid phrase is isolated, but the broader debate over the servant's identity, the exilic setting, and the poem's larger argument still remains in view. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Where the alleged fulfillment is drawn from the passion narratives, Acts, or apostolic theology, skeptics ask whether Jesus independently fulfills the line or whether his significance is being narrated through servant-song categories. That does not make the Christian reading meaningless, but it does weaken criterion 6 as evidence for stand-alone prediction.

## Original Language Notes
The language of seeing offspring and prolonging days is important and somewhat debated in its implications. It suggests continuation after suffering, but whether that continuation must be bodily resurrection is an interpretive conclusion.

## Historical and Cultural Context
In prophetic poetry, life, future, and restored longevity can function symbolically as well as literally. That wider usage cautions against assuming a single inevitable reading.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation typically understands the line within the servant's vindication or enduring future rather than as a direct prophecy of Jesus' resurrection. The verse is not usually treated as a stand-alone resurrection proof text.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians often see this as one of the most important post-suffering lines in Isaiah 53 because it appears to place life after death at the end of the servant's mission. Some treat that as direct prophecy of resurrection; others as a canonical anticipation fulfilled in Jesus.

## Textual Issues
The wording is stable enough that interpretation remains the real issue. The debate turns on what kind of continued life the verse envisions.

## My Conclusion
This claim is still meaningful within the larger Christian reading of the servant song, but under stricter historical criteria it is better classified as typological or canonical fulfillment than as a stand-alone direct prediction. The Christian case is strongest cumulatively; the skeptical case is strongest when original context, poetic ambiguity, and retrospective rereading are kept in view.
