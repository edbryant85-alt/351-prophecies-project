## Skeptic Criteria Evaluation
Criteria 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 are especially relevant because this claim interprets the servant as bearing the sins of all humanity. Skeptics argue that Isaiah 53:6a describes straying and burden-bearing in poetic corporate language, not a clearly bounded prophecy about Jesus as universal sin-bearer.

## Old Testament Passage
Isaiah 53:6 compares the people to sheep gone astray and sets up the verse's movement toward the servant carrying their burden. The image is communal and moral at the same time, emphasizing widespread human wandering.

## Old Testament Context
This verse broadens the servant song from suffering itself to the condition of those for whom the servant suffers. It is one of the most important links between human waywardness and the servant's representative role.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian interpreters connect this sub-claim with texts such as 1 John 2:2 and 4:10, which present Jesus' death as having universal atoning significance. The fulfillment claim is theological and synthetic rather than dependent on a verbal quotation.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
A skeptic will argue that the verse does not identify Jesus, define the mechanism of universal atonement, or specify the later Christian doctrine attached to it. The line may describe a collective crisis and a representative sufferer without amounting to a direct prediction of Jesus dying for all mankind.

## Original Language Notes
The sheep imagery is transparent, but the semantics of straying and burden-bearing are broader than later doctrinal formulations. The question is not whether sin is present, but how directly the text moves from collective failure to one future redeemer.

## Historical and Cultural Context
The metaphor of sheep going astray fits prophetic and pastoral imagery common in Israel's scriptures. It speaks naturally to communal failure in the exilic setting and does not require a later messianic referent to make sense.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation often reads the verse corporately and historically, with the servant related to Israel, the righteous, or another figure within the national story. It is therefore usually not read as a prophecy of Jesus bearing the sins of all humanity.

## Christian Interpretation
Christian theology sees this verse as one of the classic summaries of the human problem and the servant's representative role, ultimately fulfilled in Jesus. It is frequently read through substitutionary, representative, or canonical categories.

## Textual Issues
The text is reasonably stable. The central debate concerns scope and referent rather than a major textual variant.

## My Conclusion
This sub-claim is powerful within the full Christian reading of Isaiah 53, but it is not easy to defend as a narrow prediction on its own. It depends heavily on a larger theological framework.
