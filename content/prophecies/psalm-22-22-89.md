## Claim
Christians sometimes read Psalm 22:22 as pointing toward post-suffering vindication and, in a stronger theological reading, toward resurrection. The claim depends on the transition from deliverance to public declaration rather than on explicit resurrection vocabulary.

## Old Testament Passage
Psalm 22:22 turns from petition to proclamation: the speaker declares God's name to the assembly and praises him among the congregation. In the psalm, this is the first major sign that deliverance has been granted.

## Old Testament Context
The shift in tone after verse 21 is crucial to the psalm's structure. The sufferer who was near death is now a worshiper restored to praise, whether that restoration is read literally, liturgically, or theologically.

## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment
Christian readers connect this verse with John 20:17 and more broadly with New Testament interpretations of the risen Jesus praising God in the gathered community. The Christian link is plausible, but it is inferential and typological rather than explicit.

## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails
### Strongest Skeptical Objection
A common critical objection is that the specific claim "His Resurrection declared" comes from lament poetry, not from a passage that presents itself as a future oracle about the Messiah. From a historical-critical perspective, Psalm 22 does not function in context as a direct prediction of Jesus, even where the later Gospel overlap is striking.

### Original Meaning vs Later Interpretation
The main question is whether the New Testament use of this line is the most natural reading of Psalm 22 or a retrospective theological interpretation. The psalm originally gives voice to an afflicted worshiper in prayer; later Christian application may be profound without being identical to that original literary function.

### Ambiguity and Selective Matching
Psalm 22 uses poetic compression, metaphor, and hyperbole. A single line can sound highly specific when isolated, but the surrounding psalm moves through lament, rescue, and praise rather than through a simple report of one future execution scene. This weakens the claim under criteria 4 and 5.

### Independence / Narrative Shaping
Because the evangelists knew Psalm 22 and sometimes quote or echo it directly, skeptics ask whether the passion narratives are merely reporting fulfillment or also presenting Jesus in conscious scriptural alignment. This is one of the clearest ways criterion 6 applies to these claims.

## Original Language Notes
The language of declaring God's name in the congregation is straightforward praise language. The challenge lies in deciding how much dramatic reversal the preceding lament requires.

## Historical and Cultural Context
Thanksgiving after deliverance is a normal feature of lament-to-praise psalms. That pattern makes the verse meaningful within worship without forcing a resurrection reading.

## Jewish Interpretation
Jewish interpretation generally hears this verse as the thankful response of a sufferer who has been heard by God. It is not usually treated as an explicit resurrection prediction.

## Christian Interpretation
Christians sometimes see this verse as part of the movement from suffering to resurrection vindication. The reading often depends on the whole psalmic arc rather than on the verse in isolation.

## Textual Issues
Textual issues are limited here. The main debate is about theological scope rather than wording.

## My Conclusion
This claim can contribute to a cumulative Christian case for Psalm 22 as a passion text, but under stricter criteria it is better classified as typological or canonical fulfillment than as a stand-alone direct prediction. The correspondence may be real, yet the skeptical case becomes stronger once lament genre, poetic ambiguity, and Gospel literary shaping are taken seriously.
