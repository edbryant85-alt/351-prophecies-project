# Project Style Guide

This guide defines the working standards for the `351 Prophecies Project`. Its purpose is practical: keep pages structurally consistent, analytically fair, and readable across the whole catalog.

Reference model:
- Use [content/prophecies/isaiah-53.md](/workspaces/351-prophecies-project/content/prophecies/isaiah-53.md) as the strongest current overview-page model.
- Use the upgraded Isaiah 53, Psalm 22, Micah 5:2, Daniel 9, and Zechariah 11 claim pages as the current model for focused sub-claim analysis.

## 1. Page Types

### Overview Pages
- Scope: one major passage, chapter, or cluster of related claims.
- Purpose: explain the whole debate around a passage, not just one numbered item.
- Audience need: orientation, context, major interpretive options, and why the passage matters in the broader prophecy debate.
- Examples:
  - `isaiah-53.md`
  - `psalm-22.md`
  - `daniel-9.md`

### Claim Pages
- Scope: one numbered prophecy claim from the 351 list.
- Purpose: evaluate a specific sub-claim narrowly and precisely.
- Audience need: what the claim is, what verse it depends on, how Christians connect it to Jesus, and why skeptics object.
- Rule: claim pages support overview pages; they should not try to become mini-overviews of the entire chapter.

## 2. Required Section Structure

### Overview Pages
- Preserve this order:
  - `# [Title]`
  - overview-page notice
  - `## Related Prophecy Claims`
  - `## Claim`
  - `## Scripture Text`
  - `## 351 List Reference`
  - `## Skeptic Criteria Evaluation`
  - `## Old Testament Passage`
  - `## Old Testament Context`
  - `## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment`
  - `## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails`
  - `## Original Language Notes`
  - `## Historical and Cultural Context`
  - `## Jewish Interpretation`
  - `## Christian Interpretation`
  - `## Textual Issues`
  - `## My Conclusion`
  - `## Sources`
  - `## Copyright Notice`
- Overview pages may add criterion subheadings such as `### C2 Predictive Intent` when needed.
- Overview pages should summarize related claim pages, not duplicate them line by line.

### Claim Pages
- Preserve this order:
  - `# [Title]`
  - AI-generated placeholder notice if still in draft phase
  - backlink note to overview page
  - `## Claim`
  - `## Scripture Text`
  - `## 351 List Reference`
  - `## Skeptic Criteria Evaluation`
  - `## Old Testament Passage`
  - `## Old Testament Context`
  - `## Claimed New Testament Fulfillment`
  - `## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails`
  - `## Original Language Notes`
  - `## Historical and Cultural Context`
  - `## Jewish Interpretation`
  - `## Christian Interpretation`
  - `## Textual Issues`
  - `## My Conclusion`
  - `## Sources`
  - `## Copyright Notice`
- Claim pages should stay tightly tied to the verse or sub-claim named in the title.
- Claim pages should not re-explain the whole passage unless 1 short paragraph of local context is necessary.

## 3. Scripture Display Rules

### Overview Pages
- Include a `## Scripture Text` section after `## Claim`.
- Use selective quotation only.
- Stay within the project’s controlled verse-usage policy.
- Include:
  - 1-3 carefully chosen quotations already approved for use
  - `**Focus Verses:**` line
  - passage reference
  - Bible Gateway link
- Goal: orient the reader to the passage, not reproduce the entire chapter.

Example:
- Quote three strategically chosen lines from Isaiah 53 rather than every verse in Isaiah 52:13–53:12.

### Claim Pages
- Prefer reference-focused display.
- Use 0-1 quoted verse per page unless there is a strong reason to quote more.
- If the claim depends on only part of a verse, reference the verse and summarize carefully rather than over-quoting.
- Always include a Bible Gateway link for the full context.

### General Scripture Rules
- Use NRSVue when quoting.
- Keep attribution inline:
  - `"quoted text" (Isaiah 53:5, NRSVue)`
- Do not invent wording.
- Do not mass-copy long copyrighted blocks.
- If exact wording is uncertain, summarize rather than pretending precision.

## 4. Tone and Neutrality Guidelines

- Write like a careful researcher, not a debater.
- Aim for analytical restraint, not apologetic confidence or skeptical dismissal.
- Prefer:
  - `Many scholars argue...`
  - `Some interpreters suggest...`
  - `This depends on interpretive framework...`
  - `The connection is often treated as thematic rather than explicit.`
- Avoid:
  - `This clearly proves...`
  - `This definitively fails...`
  - `Obviously...`
  - `No honest reader could...`

### Tone Targets
- Serious
- Fair
- Specific
- Human-readable
- Non-polemical

### Practical Rule
- If a sentence sounds like it belongs in an apologetics tract or a hostile rebuttal essay, rewrite it.

## 5. Criteria Evaluation Standards

- Use the six project criteria consistently:
  - Prior
  - Predictive Intent
  - Non-Mundane
  - Specific and Verifiable
  - Clarity
  - Independence
- Do not force every page to discuss all six in equal depth.
- Focus on the criteria that actually matter for that page.

### Overview Page Standard
- State the main disputed criteria up front in `## Skeptic Criteria Evaluation`.
- In `## Why a Skeptic Says It Fails`, break out the most relevant criteria into short subsections.
- Explain the skeptical objection fairly before giving any balancing comment.

### Claim Page Standard
- Keep the criteria discussion tighter.
- Usually 2-4 criteria are enough.
- Name the main issue clearly:
  - context mismatch
  - poetic ambiguity
  - retrospective NT framing
  - weak specificity
  - lack of independence

### Example
- Good: `The strongest skeptical objection here is criterion 6, because Matthew may be shaping the narrative around Zechariah 11.`
- Weak: `Skeptics reject this because they do not believe prophecy.`

## 6. Conclusion Writing Standards

- `## My Conclusion` should be balanced, plain-language, and brief.
- The conclusion should answer:
  - How strong is this claim under strict predictive standards?
  - Does it work better cumulatively than individually?
  - Does it fit best as direct prediction, typology, canonical fulfillment, or later theological reuse?

### Overview Conclusions
- Should frame the passage as a test case for a larger prophecy model.
- Should explain why the passage matters beyond one isolated claim.
- Should avoid pretending the debate is settled.

### Claim Conclusions
- Should explicitly say whether the claim stands independently.
- In many cases, the best answer will be:
  - `This works better cumulatively than as a stand-alone prediction.`

### Recommended Conclusion Pattern
- Sentence 1: state the claim’s relative strength.
- Sentence 2: state the main limitation.
- Sentence 3: state the most plausible interpretive category.

Example:
- `As an individual claim, this is relatively weak under strict predictive criteria because the wording is broad and literary. It works better as part of a cumulative servant pattern than as a stand-alone forecast.`

## 7. Length and Scope Guidelines

### Overview Pages
- Target range: roughly 1,200-2,500 words.
- Long enough to orient the reader fully.
- Short enough to stay readable as a research overview.
- Use multiple paragraphs, but avoid turning the page into a commentary on every line.

### Claim Pages
- Target range: roughly 350-900 words.
- Shorter than overview pages in every case.
- Focus on the named claim only.
- If the analysis starts turning into a chapter overview, cut it back and move broader material to the overview page.

### Paragraph Size
- Prefer medium-length paragraphs.
- Avoid long walls of text when a section can be split into two shorter paragraphs.

### Repetition Control
- Do not copy large blocks across related claim pages.
- Reuse the same analytical logic where needed, but restate it in a way that fits the specific verse.
- Keep recurring themes concise:
  - `servant identity is debated`
  - `this may be typological rather than predictive`
  - `the Gospel narrative may be scripturally shaped`

## Quick Quality Checklist

Before considering a page draft acceptable, confirm:
- The page type is clear: overview or claim.
- Section order matches the project structure.
- The claim is stated in one clear sentence.
- Scripture handling follows project limits.
- The skeptical objection is explained fairly.
- The NT connection is described accurately:
  - explicit quotation
  - close allusion
  - thematic use
  - typological/canonical use
- The conclusion states whether the claim works independently.
- The tone is measured and non-polemical.

## Preferred Writing Pattern

Use this working pattern when drafting:
1. State the claim clearly.
2. Explain the original OT setting.
3. Explain the Christian reading.
4. Explain the skeptical objection under the relevant criteria.
5. Clarify language, textual, or historical issues.
6. End with a restrained conclusion.

If a page follows that pattern while matching the template structure, it is usually on the right track.
